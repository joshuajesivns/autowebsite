The **10th-generation Honda Civic (chassis code FC)** was the sedan that brought a genuinely quick, turbocharged RS variant to a segment that used to be all naturally-aspirated. Sold in PH from 2016 to 2021, it's now deep into the used market — and a common question from buyers is whether the turbo makes it expensive or finicky to keep alive. Short answer: **no, not really**, as long as you respect the oil and the schedule. Here's the full PMS breakdown. This is the Civic FC companion to our [guide to PMS meaning, schedule and cost](/blog/car-pms-philippines-guide).

## Key Takeaways

- Honda PH runs the same **10,000 km / 6-month** PMS cycle on the Civic FC as the City and Brio — **no shortened interval for the 1.5 Turbo**, despite the turbo.
- Two engines: **1.8L R18Z1** naturally-aspirated (141 hp/174 Nm) and **1.5L L15B7 VTEC Turbo** (173 hp/220 Nm) — both **CVT-only in PH**, no manual was ever offered.
- **Both engines use a timing chain**, not a belt — no expensive scheduled belt job to budget for.
- The 1.5 Turbo's overseas-documented **oil-dilution issue is reported as rare in PH's warmer climate**, with no local Honda recall or advisory — still worth a periodic dipstick check if you do a lot of short city trips.
- A **real, PH-specific fuel-pump recall exists** (Denso impeller) covering FC units built 2016–2019 — confirm a used unit has had the free replacement.
- **No Takata airbag recall applies** to the FC — PH Takata campaigns stop at model-year 2015.

## Which Model Year Does This Guide Cover? (2016–2021, 10th-gen FC)

This guide covers the **10th-generation Civic (FC platform)**, launched in the Philippines on **April 26, 2016** with two trims: **1.8 E CVT** and **1.5 RS Turbo CVT**. Modulo-badged variants and a limited **1.8 E CVT Navi** followed through 2016–2017, and a 30-unit **RS Turbo CVT Limited Edition** ran in 2018. A **facelift on April 15, 2019** added a cheaper entry trim, the **1.8 S CVT**, plus minor styling changes — the same two engines and CVT carried through unchanged. Note there was **never an "E/V/RS" three-trim lineup on the FC** the way later Hondas are named; that naming belongs to the 11th-gen Civic (FE), which replaced the FC in the Philippines in **November 2021**. Everything in this guide applies across the full 2016–2021 span.

One thing this guide **doesn't** cover: the **Civic Type R (FK8)**, a separate 100-unit limited run sold in PH starting 2017 with its own 2.0L turbo, 6-speed manual, and a completely different maintenance and parts profile from the regular FC. If you're after Type R content, that's a future JDM-vertical topic, not a PMS-cost one.

Honda's own official PM Cost Calculator is currently offline, and the only hard peso figure we could verify is an older, RS Turbo-specific data point from 2017 (₱3,810.55 at 10,000 km, ₱15,849.20 at 40,000 km) — useful only as a directional reference, since real prices today are almost certainly higher after years of inflation and parts-cost increases. **Always confirm current pricing with your Honda dealer.** For what's actually done at each interval, see the [full car maintenance cost guide](/blog/car-pms-philippines-guide).

## Light PMS vs. Heavy PMS on the Civic FC

Most visits are **light PMS** — oil and filter plus a general inspection, every 10,000 km, on either engine. The **heavy (major) PMS at 40,000 km and 80,000 km** is where costs step up: brake fluid, coolant, air and fuel filters, spark plugs, and — critically — the **CVT fluid (genuine HCF-2)**. As with every Honda CVT in this lineup, that fluid service is not optional; skipping it is the single fastest way to shorten the gearbox's life.

## Known Issues on the Honda Civic FC

- **1.5 Turbo oil dilution — a real global issue, reported as rare here.** The L15B7 turbo engine's oil-dilution problem is well documented in the US and Canada (Honda even extended warranty coverage there). Philippine sources report it as uncommon locally, likely due to the warmer year-round climate, and Honda Cars Philippines has issued no local recall or TSB for it. Still worth periodically checking your oil level and smell, especially if your driving is mostly short city trips that don't fully warm the engine.
- **Fuel-pump recall (2016–2019 units, confirmed PH-specific).** A Denso fuel-pump impeller defect that can cause stalling or failure to start was covered across several Honda Cars Philippines recall rounds (2020, 2021, and a final breakdown as recent as November 2024) — affecting roughly 947 units of the 1.5 Turbo (2016–2019) and 2,427 units of the 1.8L (2016–2018). The fix is a free replacement. If buying used, ask for proof it was done.
- **No Takata airbag recall applies.** Unlike some older Honda models, PH Takata campaigns are capped at model-year 2001–2015 — the FC (2016 onward) is not included.
- **Missing rear aircon vents on base/mid trims.** A real, often-repeated complaint in a tropical market — the 1.8 E and S trims skip rear vents that some rivals include. A design choice, not a defect, but worth knowing before you buy for backseat comfort.
- **Normal CVT behavior.** A mild "rubber-band" feel under hard acceleration is typical Honda-CVT character, not a fault — persistent hesitation or shudder usually just means the HCF-2 fluid is overdue.

## Casa vs. Talyer & Parts

Under warranty → **Honda casa**, and insist on genuine HCF-2 for any CVT service. Out of warranty → a trusted talyer is usually cheaper for general work, provided they use the correct fluids and, on the turbo, quality oil on schedule. If you're cross-shopping Honda's smaller sedan too, see our [Honda City ownership-cost breakdown](/blog/honda-city-pms-guide).

## Verdict

The Civic FC — especially the 1.5 Turbo RS — remains one of the more satisfying daily drivers of its generation to own, and it doesn't punish you for that with a scary maintenance bill: same 10,000 km interval as Honda's other models, no timing belt to worry about, and only one recall to check off on a used unit. The habits that matter are simple — genuine HCF-2 on the CVT, quality oil on the turbo, and don't skip a service just because the car still feels fine. Do that, and it's a strong, still-relevant used buy years after the 11th-gen replaced it.
