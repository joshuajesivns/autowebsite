The **Toyota Raize** is the compact crossover that gives first-time buyers SUV styling and a turbocharged punch in a tiny, efficient package. Maintaining it is affordable and close to a subcompact sedan, pero may isang bagay ang turbo na hindi mo dapat kalimutan: oil discipline. Here's exactly what a Raize costs to service, interval by interval, plus the items and known issues to watch. This is the Raize companion to our [complete Car PMS guide](/blog/car-pms-philippines-guide).

## Key Takeaways

- The Raize runs the standard **5,000 km / 6-month** PMS cycle.
- A regular Raize PMS at casa is about **₱5,000–7,500**; a major 40K–80K service is **₱10,000–15,000+**. *(Average casa prices, June 2026 — verify with your Toyota dealer.)*
- **Turbo discipline:** use quality oil and never stretch the interval — the 1.0L turbo is less forgiving of dirty oil than an NA engine.
- The **D-CVT** benefits from a fluid change ~every 40,000 km for long-term smoothness.
- **Timing chain, not belt** = walang mahal na belt replacement later.

## Toyota Raize PMS Cost by Interval

Costs climb at the bigger intervals when more than oil gets replaced. Ganito ang dating sa casa:

| PMS stage | Interval | Casa cost (Raize) |
|---|---|---|
| First PMS | 1,000–5,000 km | ₱2,000–4,000 *(usually free labor)* |
| Standard PMS | 10,000–40,000 km | ₱5,000–7,500 |
| Major PMS | 40,000–80,000 km | ₱10,000–15,000+ |

The Raize sits in the subcompact range. The turbo doesn't make routine PMS dramatically pricier — it just raises the stakes on doing it *on time* with the right oil. For the full interval-by-interval breakdown of *what's actually done* at each stop, see the [main PMS guide](/blog/car-pms-philippines-guide).

## Common Wear Items on a Raize

A solid little crossover, pero may babantayan — lalo na sa turbo:

- **Engine oil (turbo)** — quality oil, changed on schedule, is the single most important thing for the 1.0L turbo's longevity.
- **D-CVT fluid** — change it ~every 40,000 km for long-term smoothness.
- **Brake pads, wipers, battery** — normal wear; cheap and easy.
- **Pang-ilalim** — bushings, tie rod ends, and stabilizer links take a beating on PH roads. Listen for `lagutok` and `kalog` — full sound guide in the [PMS pillar](/blog/car-pms-philippines-guide).
- **Aircon** — PH heat works the system hard; expect a clean/recharge as it ages.

## Known Issues on the Raize

Mostly characteristics to set expectations, not red flags:

- **Daihatsu origin** — the Raize is built on a Daihatsu platform (shared with the Perodua/Daihatsu Rocky). It's well-proven, but pre-owned buyers sometimes ask about the 2023–2024 Daihatsu testing-certification issue that briefly affected some models abroad; check that your specific unit's papers and any recall actions are clean.
- **Small turbo means oil sensitivity** — covered above, but worth repeating: this is the one habit that defines long-term reliability.
- **Firm-ish ride** — the short wheelbase and small car can feel busy on rough roads; normal, not a fault.

## Casa vs. Talyer for a Raize

**Under warranty → Toyota casa** (keep your receipts). **Out of warranty → a trusted talyer** is usually cheaper for the same work — but for the turbo, make sure the shop uses the correct oil spec.

Toyota's network is the densest in PH, so casa visits and parts are about as convenient as it gets.

## What You Can DIY

Between visits: check tire pressure, top up washer fluid, swap the cabin filter, and eyeball your oil level. Leave CVT fluid, turbo-related work, and warranty service to the pros.

## Verdict: A Turbo Crossover That's Cheap to Keep

The Raize gives you SUV looks and turbo pep for the money, with affordable routine PMS, the Toyota service network, and a timing chain (no belt drama). The deal: treat oil changes as non-negotiable on the turbo, and keep your records clean.
