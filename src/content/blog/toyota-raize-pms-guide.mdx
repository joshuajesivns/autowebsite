The **Toyota Raize** is the compact crossover that gives first-time buyers SUV styling and turbocharged punch in a tiny, efficient package. Maintaining it is affordable and close to a subcompact sedan, pero may isang bagay ang turbo na hindi mo dapat kalimutan: oil discipline. Here's exactly what a Raize costs to service, interval by interval, plus the items, the recall, and known issues to watch. This is the Raize companion to our [guide to PMS meaning, schedule and cost](/blog/car-pms-philippines-guide).

## Key Takeaways

- The Raize runs the standard **5,000 km / 6-month** PMS cycle.
- A light/standard Raize PMS at casa is about **₱5,000–7,500**; a **heavy/major** 40K–80K service is **₱10,000–15,000+**. *(Average casa prices, June 2026 — verify with your Toyota dealer.)*
- **Two engines:** a 1.0L turbo (1KR-VET) on the top variant and a 1.2L naturally-aspirated (WA-VE) on the rest — know which one you're buying.
- **Turbo discipline:** on the 1.0L turbo, use quality oil and never stretch the interval — it's less forgiving of dirty oil than an NA engine.
- **A real PH recall exists** (Feb 2025, free ECU reprogramming) — make sure any used unit has had it done.
- **Timing chain, not belt** = walang mahal na belt replacement later.

## Which Model Year Does This Guide Cover? (2022–2026)

This guide covers the Toyota Raize since its Philippine launch on February 4, 2022 — the only generation sold here to date, spanning the 2022 through 2026 model years with no structural facelift, just trim and price adjustments. Both engines covered here, the 1.0L turbo and 1.2L naturally-aspirated, have been offered across that entire span. If you're shopping a used unit built as early as December 2021 (pre-launch production stock, some of it covered by the Feb 2025 recall), the same information applies. One clarification worth repeating for used-market shoppers: the 2023–24 Daihatsu certification scandal in Japan never touched the PH-spec Raize, which is built in Indonesia and was never subject to a sales or production halt here.

## Toyota Raize Quick Specs

| Spec | Detail |
|---|---|
| Engines | 1.0L (998cc) 1KR-VET 3-cyl turbo · 1.2L (1,198cc) WA-VE 3-cyl, naturally aspirated |
| Power | 1.0 Turbo: 98 hp / 140 Nm · 1.2 NA: ~88 hp / 113 Nm |
| Oil capacity | ~3.1 L (1.0 turbo) / ~3.4 L (1.2 NA), with filter |
| Oil grade | 0W-20 (1.0 turbo) / 0W-16 (1.2 NA) — check your manual |
| Transmission | CVT (1.0 Turbo & 1.2 CVT) or 5-speed manual (1.2 E MT) |
| Timing | Chain (no scheduled belt change) |
| PMS interval | Every 5,000 km or 6 months |

*The PH Raize is gasoline-only (no hybrid here) and built in Indonesia. The 1.0 Turbo uses Daihatsu's D-CVT; its dedicated CVT fluid is typically replaced at the 80,000 km service.*

## Toyota Raize PMS Cost by Interval

Costs climb at the bigger intervals when more than oil gets replaced. Ganito ang dating sa casa:

| PMS stage | Interval | Casa cost (Raize) |
|---|---|---|
| First PMS | 1,000–5,000 km | ₱2,000–4,000 *(usually free labor)* |
| Light / standard PMS | 10,000–30,000 km | ₱5,000–7,500 |
| Heavy / major PMS | 40,000–80,000 km | ₱10,000–15,000+ |

The Raize sits in the subcompact range. The turbo doesn't make routine PMS dramatically pricier — it just raises the stakes on doing it *on time* with the right oil. ## Light PMS vs. Heavy PMS on the Raize

Most Raize visits are **light PMS** — engine oil + filter and a general inspection, every 5,000 km. On the 1.0L turbo this is the single most important habit for longevity: quality oil, changed on schedule.

The **heavy (major) PMS** at **40,000 km and 80,000 km** adds brake fluid, coolant, the air-cleaner element, spark plugs, and — at 80,000 km — the **CVT fluid**, plus a full inspection of the suspension and bushings. The CVT uses a dedicated Toyota fluid, not generic ATF; using the wrong fluid is a real damage risk, so leave it to the casa.

## Known Issues on the Raize

Mostly characteristics and one real recall — worth knowing, especially on the used market:

- **Feb 2025 ECU/brake recall (PH).** Toyota Motor Philippines recalled ~28,828 Raize units built **Dec 2021 – Jul 2024** for improper engine-ECU programming that, under certain conditions, could reduce brake-booster suction pressure and lengthen stopping distance. The fix is a **free ECU reprogramming**. Confirm a used unit has had it applied — it's unrelated to the overseas Daihatsu issue below.
- **The Daihatsu certification scandal did *not* affect the PH Raize.** The 2023–24 Daihatsu cert-testing irregularities halted some Japan-built models, but the PH Raize is **built in Indonesia** and was cleared — no PH sales or production halt occurred. The Raize HEV airbag re-test was Japan-domestic only; PH never got the hybrid.
- **Small turbo means oil sensitivity** — on the 1.0L turbo, clean oil changed on time is non-negotiable; dirty or stretched oil is the fastest way to shorten a small turbo's life.
- **Firm-ish ride** — the short wheelbase and small body can feel busy on rough roads; normal, not a fault.

## Casa vs. Talyer for a Raize

**Under warranty → Toyota casa** (keep your receipts). **Out of warranty → a trusted talyer** is usually cheaper for the same work — but for the turbo, make sure the shop uses the correct oil spec.

Toyota's network is the densest in PH, so casa visits and parts are about as convenient as it gets.

## What You Can DIY

Between visits: check tire pressure, top up washer fluid, swap the cabin filter, and eyeball your oil level. Leave CVT fluid, turbo-related work, and warranty/recall service to the pros.

## Verdict: A Turbo Crossover That's Cheap to Keep

The Raize gives you SUV looks and turbo pep for the money, with affordable routine PMS, the Toyota service network, and a timing chain (no belt drama). The deal: treat oil changes as non-negotiable on the turbo, make sure the recall's been done, and keep your records clean.
