Few nameplates carry the weight the **Toyota Hilux** does in the Philippines. Since it officially arrived in **1988** — taking over from Toyota's earlier **Stout** and **Briska** pickups — it's gone from bare-bones workhorse to lifestyle truck without ever losing the reputation that made it: *tibay*. That long history is great news for a used buyer, but it also means "a Hilux" can be anything from a 1990s mechanical-diesel beater to a modern common-rail truck with a DPF. This guide walks every generation sold here, what each is worth, and — most importantly — **what to check before you pay**. It sits alongside our [modern Hilux service guide](/blog/toyota-hilux-pms-guide) and the [older D-4D diesel maintenance breakdown](/blog/toyota-hilux-vigo-diesel-pms-guide).

## Key Takeaways

- **This guide covers every PH generation from the 1988 arrival to the 2026 all-new model** — five generations, each a different truck to own.
- **The used sweet spot for most people is the 7th-gen Vigo (2005–2015)** — tough, cheap to fix, and understood by every talyer.
- **Bigger budget? The 8th-gen Revo (2015–2025)** brings modern safety, power, and the Conquest/GR Sport trims.
- **The single biggest tech split is timing belt vs. chain:** D-4D Vigo/Tiger diesels use a **belt** (replace ~150,000 km); the modern Revo uses a **chain**.
- **Condition and records beat model year.** A well-kept older unit can be a smarter buy than a neglected newer one.
- **Non-negotiable used checks:** service history, timing-belt record (D-4D), injector health, chassis rust, flood history, working 4x4, and clean LTO papers.
- **New for 2026:** an all-new 9th-gen Hilux and a fully electric Hilux BEV have arrived — but the proven-quality pick today is still the late 8th-gen.

## Fifth Generation (1988–1997): The Original Workhorse

This is where the Hilux story starts in the Philippines. The fifth-gen was pure utility — leaf-sprung, body-on-frame, and built to be fixed anywhere with basic tools. Its diesels were **older mechanical (indirect-injection) units**, not common-rail, which means no sensitive electronics but also more smoke and noise by modern standards. Today these are cheap — think project trucks, farm duty, or a first "matibay lang" beater. **What to watch:** rust and chassis integrity are the real enemies at this age, and originality of the drivetrain. Buy one for character and toughness, not comfort.

## Sixth Generation / "Tiger" (1997–2005)

The **Tiger** is a genuine PH legend, locally assembled in Sta. Rosa, Laguna. Its engine range is the most varied of any Hilux era here: the **5L 3.0L mechanical diesel** (famous for being nearly indestructible), the **1KZ-TE 3.0L turbo-diesel** (electronically controlled but *not* common-rail — a sweet spot of durability and a bit more power), and, toward the end of the run, the early **2.5L 2KD-FTV D-4D** common-rail engine. **What to watch:** the mechanical diesels are simple and hardy but tired examples smoke and leak; the 1KZ-TE is prized, so verify it's healthy and original; any late 2KD D-4D brings the timing-belt and injector considerations covered below. Still one of the best value-for-toughness buys if you find a cared-for unit.

## Seventh Generation / "Vigo" (2005–2015)

For most used buyers, the **Vigo is the sweet spot.** It was the first fully **D-4D common-rail** Hilux — the **2.5L 2KD-FTV** and **3.0L 1KD-FTV** — with a big enough production run that parts are everywhere and every mechanic knows it. It's modern enough to daily comfortably but old enough to be affordable. Two era-specific things define ownership: it uses a **timing belt** (replace around 150,000 km) and its common-rail **injectors** are the classic watch item. We cover both in depth in the [classic Hilux diesel maintenance guide](/blog/toyota-hilux-vigo-diesel-pms-guide) — read it before buying one. **What to watch:** timing-belt records, injector health, and the usual chassis/flood checks.

## Eighth Generation / "Revo" (2015–2025)

The **Revo** is the modern Hilux most people picture today — the **2.4L 2GD-FTV** and **2.8L 1GD-FTV** diesels, a **timing chain** (no belt job), and a **DPF**. It brought a big jump in safety, refinement, and tech, plus the lifestyle **Conquest** trims and the range-topping **GR Sport**. A **facelift in September 2020** sharpened the styling and bumped the 2.8 to 204 PS / 500 Nm. It's the newest truck with a real ownership track record, which makes a clean late-model Revo the safest "modern Hilux" used buy right now. Maintenance specifics are in our [current Hilux PMS guide](/blog/toyota-hilux-pms-guide). **What to watch:** DPF health on pure-city units, the early 1GD's low-speed hesitation calibration fix (2016–2018), and service records.

## Ninth Generation (2026–): All-New, Plus the Hilux BEV

The **all-new ninth-generation Hilux arrived in the Philippines in June 2026**, now standard on the 2.8L GD diesel and — a first — joined by a fully electric **Hilux BEV**. It's exciting, but brand new: there's no long-term reliability or used-value data yet, and early-run trucks always carry a little more unknown. If you want the newest thing and buy new, go for it; if you're shopping used for proven quality, the late 8th-gen is still the known quantity.

## Which Hilux Generation Should You Buy?

- **Tightest budget / project / farm-work:** 5th gen or a mechanical-diesel Tiger. Buy on chassis condition, not looks.
- **Best toughness-per-peso:** a healthy **1KZ-TE Tiger** — simple, strong, cheap to keep.
- **The all-rounder most people should buy:** a well-kept **Vigo (2005–2015)**, 2.5 for economy or 3.0 for pulling power. Modern enough, cheap to run, parts everywhere.
- **Modern safety, power, and comfort:** a late **Revo (2015–2025)** — Conquest if you want the lifestyle kit, GR Sport if you want the range-topper. Planning to build it out? See how the [GR Sport and an overland build come together](/blog/toyota-hilux-gr-sport-overland-build).
- **Brand-new, latest tech (or going electric):** the 2026 ninth-gen or the Hilux BEV — buy new, and let the used data mature.

## The Used Hilux Buying Checklist

Whatever the generation, run this before you pay:

- **Service records + ownership history.** One careful owner with casa/talyer receipts is worth paying more for.
- **Timing belt record (D-4D Vigo/Tiger).** Ask when it was last replaced. No record on a 150,000 km+ truck = assume overdue and budget the job.
- **Injector & cold-start health.** Start it stone-cold: listen for heavy knocking and watch for excessive black or white smoke that doesn't clear.
- **Chassis & frame.** It's body-on-frame — get underneath and check for heavy rust, cracks, or crude repairs, especially on coastal/flood-prone units.
- **Flood history.** Musty cabin smell, water lines under carpets/seats, corroded connectors, or fogged lights are red flags in a typhoon country.
- **4x4 function.** On 4x4 units, engage 4H and 4L and confirm it actually drives — repairs here are pricey.
- **Transmission & clutch.** Smooth AT shifts; a clutch that isn't slipping or grabbing at the very top on the manual.
- **Papers & identity.** Chassis and engine numbers must match the OR/CR, registration current with the LTO, and any bank encumbrance / chattel mortgage fully cleared. Walk away from murky documents.

A one-time inspection at a diesel specialist before buying is the cheapest insurance you'll ever get on a used Hilux.

## Diesel vs. Gas, 4x2 vs. 4x4

The Hilux is a **diesel truck at heart** in the Philippines — the vast majority sold here are diesel, which is why resale, parts, and mechanic know-how all favor it (gas variants existed but are rarer and less sought-after used). On drivetrain, **4x2** is lighter, cheaper, and more economical for pure city/highway and light loads; **4x4** costs more to buy and maintain but is worth it for provincial roads, job sites, and real off-road — just make sure the system works before you pay the 4x4 premium.

## Verdict: Buy the Record, Not Just the Year

The Hilux's decades in the Philippines mean there's a right one for almost every budget — from a ₱250,000 Tiger that'll outlive you to a nearly-new Revo. But the single most important lesson across all of them is the same: **condition and paperwork beat model year every time.** Find the generation that fits your budget and use, then buy the specific truck with the cleanest history, the healthiest engine, and the clearest papers. Do that, and you'll understand exactly why "Hilux" became shorthand for a truck that just won't quit.
