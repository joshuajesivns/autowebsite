The **Mitsubishi Destinator** is the brand's newest play for Filipino families: a proper three-row, seven-seat SUV that slots above the Xpander but below the diesel Montero Sport. What makes it different from anything Mitsubishi has sold here before is under the hood and in the gearbox — a **1.5-liter turbocharged gas engine** paired with a **CVT**, a combination the Philippine Xpander never used. That newness is exactly what raises the ownership question: magkano ba to imaintain, and does a turbo-plus-CVT setup mean a bigger service bill? Short answer — no, and there's a pleasant surprise in the schedule. Here's the full breakdown, interval by interval, with the timing-chain answer, what the turbo and CVT actually add, the warranty, and an honest note on what's *not* yet known about a car this fresh. This is the Destinator companion to our [complete guide to PMS meaning, schedule and cost](/blog/car-pms-philippines-guide).

## Key Takeaways

- **PMS schedule:** the Destinator runs a **10,000 km / 6-month** cycle per Mitsubishi PH's official schedule — **double the Xpander's 5,000 km interval**, and the same cadence as the Montero Sport. Oil + filter each stop; heavy/major service at **40,000 km and 80,000 km**. Full chart below.
- A **light/standard PMS** should be about **₱4,500–7,000**; the heavy/major service is **₱10,000–16,000+**. *(Estimates — no Destinator-specific casa price list exists yet; based on the 1.5 turbo engine + CVT. Verify with your Mitsubishi dealer, July 2026.)*
- **Turbo + CVT is a Mitsubishi PH first** for this class — the 4B40 1.5T makes **163 PS (161 hp) / 250 Nm**, driving a **JATCO CVT**, front-wheel drive only (no manual, no AWD).
- **Three variants:** GLX (₱1,389,000), GLS (₱1,599,000), and the top GT (₱1,929,000) — current 2026 SRPs.
- **Timing belt or chain?** **Chain** on the 4B40 — no scheduled belt replacement later.
- **Too new for a known-issues list, and no PH recall on record** as of July 2026. Backed by a **5-star ASEAN NCAP** rating and a **5-year warranty**.

## Which Model Year Does This Guide Cover? (2025–2026)

This guide covers the Mitsubishi Destinator from its Philippine launch on **November 20, 2025**. It's a brand-new nameplate — the first generation — and nothing has changed in spec or lineup since launch; the "2026 model year" simply labels the current car. The Destinator is **CBU-imported from Indonesia** (Mitsubishi's Cikarang plant, West Java), not locally assembled, so it's worth knowing that some body panels and model-specific trim can take a little longer to source than the everywhere-common Xpander (routine consumables — oil, filters — are not affected). If you're cross-shopping, be clear on where it sits: it rides on a **monocoque front-wheel-drive platform** shared with the Xforce and Xpander — it is *not* a ladder-frame off-roader like the Montero Sport.

## Mitsubishi Destinator Maintenance Schedule (PMS Interval Chart)

Here's the headline that saves you money: the Destinator follows Mitsubishi PH's **every 10,000 km or 6 months** PMS cadence — **not** the 5,000 km cycle the Xpander runs. That means roughly half as many trips to the casa over the same distance. Oil and filter plus an inspection happen at every stop, with the bigger-ticket items landing at the 40,000 km and 80,000 km "heavy" services. Ganito ang buong schedule ng *kung ano ang ginagawa* sa bawat interval:

| Odometer (km) | PMS type | Key items done |
|---|---|---|
| 1,000 | First check (free) | First inspection, oil level + bolt-torque check, no major parts |
| 10,000 | Light | Engine oil + oil filter, general inspection |
| 20,000 | Light | Oil + filter, tire rotation, brake inspection |
| 30,000 | Light | Oil + filter, air-cleaner element check, general inspection |
| **40,000** | **Heavy / major** | Oil + filter, **brake fluid, coolant, spark plugs, air + fuel filters**, CVT-fluid inspection, full inspection |
| 50,000–70,000 | Light | Oil + filter each 10k/6-mo stop, rotating inspections |
| **80,000** | **Heavy / major** | Everything at 40K **+ CVT fluid service**, suspension + bushing inspection |

*Whichever comes first — km or months. One honest caveat for PH conditions: 10,000 km is the official oil interval, but this is a **turbocharged** engine in Manila/South Luzon heat and traffic, which Mitsubishi itself treats as "severe" service. Plenty of turbo owners change oil a bit sooner (every 6 months regardless of distance, or ~7,500 km) for extra insurance — never a bad idea, but the 10,000 km / 6-month schedule is the spec.*

## Light PMS vs. Heavy PMS on the Destinator

Most Destinator visits are **light PMS** — engine oil + filter and a general check, every 10,000 km. They're quick and affordable because the small turbo engine only takes about four liters of oil.

The **heavy (major) PMS** lands at **40,000 km and 80,000 km**. On top of the oil change, the casa replaces the items that only come due occasionally — air and fuel filters, brake fluid, coolant, spark plugs, and, crucially on this car, the **CVT fluid** (inspected at 40K, serviced by 80K). That's why the major-service bill roughly doubles a light PMS. Budget for those two stops in advance para hindi ka ma-shock — but note they're still modest next to a diesel SUV's major service.

## Mitsubishi Destinator: Timing Belt or Chain?

**Chain.** The Destinator's **4B40 1.5-liter turbo** engine is a chain-driven design, which means there's **no scheduled timing-belt replacement** (the ₱15,000–30,000+ job that belt-driven cars need around 100,000 km). One caveat worth respecting: the 4B40 is an **interference engine**, so if a chain were ever neglected into failure, the valves and pistons can meet — expensive. A chain is built to last the engine's life on clean oil, so the single best thing you can do is keep the oil changes on time. *(The chain design is confirmed at the 4B40 engine-family level; no Destinator-specific PH spec sheet prints the chain-vs-belt line, but the entire 4B40 range is timing-chain.)*

## The Turbo + CVT Combo: What It Actually Adds

This is the section Xpander owners will care about most, because the Destinator's turbo-and-CVT drivetrain is genuinely new for a Mitsubishi 7-seater here. The good news: it adds very little to your maintenance, as long as you respect two things.

- **The turbo runs on your engine oil — so oil quality and timing matter more.** The turbocharger is lubricated and cooled by the same oil circuit, spinning at very high rpm and temperature. Use the correct **0W-20 full-synthetic** (API SP / ILSAC GF-6) and don't stretch changes, and the turbo is effectively maintenance-free. A brief cool-down idle after hard highway running is a good old-school habit, though modern water-cooled turbos are forgiving.
- **The dual-injection setup is a plus, not a worry.** Pure direct-injection engines can build carbon on the intake valves over time; the 4B40 adds **port injection** alongside direct, which helps keep those valves clean. It's one less long-term headache than a GDI-only turbo.
- **The CVT fluid is the one genuinely new item vs. the Xpander.** The Destinator uses a **JATCO CVT** (the same family Nissan uses). Unlike the Xpander's simple 4-speed automatic, a CVT wants its fluid inspected and eventually changed — that's factored into the 40K/80K major services above. Don't stretch it, especially with a full seven-seat load in stop-go traffic, which is the hardest life a CVT can have.
- **Should you worry about the CVT?** JATCO CVTs have a mixed reputation online, but the reported problems are largely tied to heavier-duty units in bigger vehicles, and there are **no Destinator-specific failure reports** — it's simply too new. Treat it like any CVT: keep the fluid fresh, avoid abuse, and it should be fine. We'll update this guide if a real pattern emerges here.

Bottom line: the turbo asks for on-time quality oil, and the CVT asks for on-time fluid. Do both and the Destinator services almost as simply as a normal gas SUV — with more power and better economy than a naturally-aspirated 7-seater for the trouble.

## Known Issues on the Destinator — Honestly, It's Too New to Say

We won't invent a known-issues list to fill this section. The Destinator has only been on sale in the Philippines since November 2025, so there is **no credible body of owner-defect data yet**, and — importantly — **no recall on record** for it as of July 2026. That's genuinely reassuring for an early buyer, but it also means long-term reliability is still unproven here.

What *does* exist so far is minor reviewer and early-owner commentary, not defects:

- **Occasional interior plastic noise.** A few early owners noted minor cabin creaks; dealers have described it as normal break-in, not a fault.
- **Head-unit lag.** The large touchscreen can feel a touch slow to some reviewers — a software nitpick, not a mechanical issue.
- **Cosmetic nitpicks.** Minor panel-gap and wheel-size preferences turned up in a couple of reviews — subjective, not reliability.

Treat these as first-impression notes. We'll update this guide as real ownership data accumulates — and if a recall or a genuine pattern emerges, it'll go here, verified.

## Mitsubishi Destinator Warranty

Every Destinator ships with **5 years of warranty coverage** in the Philippines — Mitsubishi PH's standard **3-year bumper-to-bumper** warranty plus a **2-year extension bundled specifically on this model**. That's a strong safety net for a brand-new nameplate and a reason to keep every PMS at the casa during the coverage years — dealer-stamped service records are usually what keep a warranty valid. *(No exact mileage cap is published for the PH warranty, so we quote it in years only; ignore any "100,000 km" figure you see, which comes from US/Canada Mitsubishi materials that don't apply here. ## Casa vs. Talyer & Parts

Easy call while it's new: **under warranty → Mitsubishi casa** (keep your receipts) — and for the CVT and turbo specifically, warranty-era work belongs at the casa regardless. **Out of warranty → a trusted talyer** is usually cheaper for routine gas-engine work, though CVT-fluid service is best left to a shop that knows JATCO units and uses the correct fluid. Mitsubishi's dealer network and parts availability in PH are strong — not quite Toyota-dense, but solid. The one caveat, as noted, is that the Destinator is **CBU-imported from Indonesia**, so model-specific body panels and trim can take longer to source than the locally-common Xpander. Between visits you can DIY the basics — tire pressure, washer fluid, cabin filter, and an oil-level eyeball — and leave CVT fluid, turbo, and warranty/recall work to the pros.

## Safety: A Strong Start

For a family SUV, the Destinator's safety story is a genuine selling point. It earned a **5-star ASEAN NCAP rating** (2025 test, 77.27 points), and Mitsubishi's driver-assist suite, **Mitsubishi Motors Safety Sensing**, is offered — though it's **trim-gated**, so know what you're buying:

- **GLX** — reverse camera plus the ABS/EBD/stability-control basics; four airbags.
- **GLS** — adds a 360° camera, Blind Spot Warning, Rear Cross Traffic Alert, and front/rear sensors; still four airbags, and *no* adaptive cruise or lane-keep.
- **GT** — the full suite: Adaptive Cruise Control, Forward Collision Mitigation (autonomous emergency braking), Lead Car Departure Notification, and Auto High Beam, plus curtain airbags for six total.

If active driver-assist matters to you, the GT is the variant that has it; the GLX and GLS keep the passive safety and cameras but not the radar-based features.

## Verdict: A Roomy Turbo Family SUV That's Cheap to Keep

If you want seven seats, real SUV presence, and turbocharged pull without stepping up to a diesel service bill, the Destinator makes a strong case. The headline for owners is that **10,000 km / 6-month schedule** — half the casa trips of an Xpander — on a small gas engine with a timing chain (no belt drama) and dual injection that fights carbon buildup. The turbo just wants on-time quality oil, and the CVT just wants its fluid kept fresh; do both and running costs stay reasonable. The honest asterisks are that it's new (unproven long-term here, no published PMS price sheet yet) and CBU-imported (trim parts can take longer) — but a 5-star crash rating and a 5-year warranty are a confident start. Keep to the schedule, budget for the 40K/80K major services, and keep your records clean.
