The **Toyota Hilux** is the default diesel pickup of the Philippines — a body-on-frame workhorse trusted equally by businesses, provincial families, and off-roaders, and the truck that turned "Hilux" into shorthand for *tibay*. It costs a little less to service than a Fortuner (pickup, not SUV) but more than a gas sedan, kasi diesel at malaki ang makina. Here's exactly what a Hilux costs to maintain, interval by interval, plus the full PMS schedule, the timing-chain answer, the DPF care it needs, and the known issues to watch. This is the Hilux companion to our [complete Philippine car maintenance guide](/blog/car-pms-philippines-guide).

## Key Takeaways

- **PMS schedule:** the Hilux runs the standard **5,000 km / 6-month** diesel cycle — oil + filter each light PMS, with heavy/major service at **40,000 km and 80,000 km**. Full chart below.
- A light/standard Hilux PMS at casa is about **₱6,000–10,000**; a **heavy/major** 40K–80K service is **₱15,000–25,000+**. *(Average casa estimates, July 2026 — verify with your Toyota dealer.)*
- **Two diesels:** a 2.4L (2GD-FTV, 150 PS) on J/E/G variants and a 2.8L (1GD-FTV, 204 PS) on Conquest 4x4 and GR Sport — both take pricier diesel-spec oil than a gas vehicle.
- **Timing belt or chain?** **Chain** — no scheduled belt replacement later.
- **DPF, clean diesel, fuel filter, and low-ash (DL-1) oil** are the items that keep it healthy long-term.
- **Class-leading resale** — the Hilux and Ranger trade the pickup sales crown, and the Hilux's resale is among the strongest in the segment, which offsets the diesel running cost.
- **New for 2026:** an all-new 9th-gen Hilux (and a fully electric Hilux BEV) arrived in June 2026 — this guide covers the 8th-gen truck almost everyone owns today, with the new one flagged where it matters.

## Which Model Year Does This Guide Cover? (2015–2026)

This guide covers the **eighth-generation Toyota Hilux** (the "Revo," chassis AN120/AN130), on sale in the Philippines since 2015. It received a major facelift in **2020** that gave it the bolder front end and, importantly, retuned the 2.8L 1GD-FTV diesel: pre-facelift (2015–2020) the 2.8 made about **177 PS / 450 Nm**, while the facelift bumped it to **204 PS / 500 Nm** on the automatic. The lifestyle **Conquest** trims and the range-topping **GR Sport** (suspension, styling, and chassis upgrades) arrived within this generation. Everything in this guide — both diesels, the timing chain, the DPF — applies across the full 2015–2026 eighth-gen range, pre- or post-facelift.

One honest heads-up: the **all-new, ninth-generation Hilux arrived in the Philippines in June 2026** (unveiled at PIMS), now standard on the 2.8L GD diesel (204 hp, 420/500 Nm) and joined by a fully electric **Hilux BEV**. Because it's brand new, there isn't real owner or long-term service data for it yet — so we won't invent ninth-gen-specific figures here. The good news: it stays in the **same GD-series diesel family** and Toyota PH's diesel PMS cadence is unchanged, so the fundamentals below (5,000 km oil intervals, DPF care, timing chain, DL-1 oil) carry over. We'll expand this guide with verified ninth-gen specifics once they're available.

## Toyota Hilux Maintenance Schedule (PMS Interval Chart)

The Hilux follows Toyota's standard **every 5,000 km or 6 months** PMS cadence — diesel oil + filter plus an inspection at every stop, with the bigger-ticket diesel items landing at the 40,000 km and 80,000 km "heavy" services. Here's the full schedule of *what actually gets done* at each interval:

| Odometer (km) | PMS type | Key items done |
|---|---|---|
| 1,000 | First check (free) | First inspection, oil level + bolt-torque check, no major parts |
| 5,000 | Light | Diesel engine oil + oil filter, general inspection |
| 10,000 | Light | Oil + filter, tire rotation, brake inspection |
| 15,000 | Light | Oil + filter, general inspection |
| 20,000 | Light | Oil + filter, air-cleaner element check, fuel/water-separator drain |
| 30,000 | Light | Oil + filter, brake + suspension inspection |
| **40,000** | **Heavy / major** | Oil + filter, **fuel filter, brake fluid, coolant, air filter**, transmission + differential fluid check, full diesel-system + DPF check |
| 50,000–70,000 | Light | Oil + filter each 5k/6-mo stop, rotating inspections |
| **80,000** | **Heavy / major** | Everything at 40K **+ transmission fluid, transfer/diff oil (4x4)**, suspension + bushing inspection |

*Whichever comes first — km or months. Note there are **no spark plugs** to replace here — it's a diesel; the equivalent snippet-worthy items are the fuel filter and DPF. For dusty province routes, farm/site work, or heavy stop-go traffic, Toyota treats the schedule as "severe," so don't stretch the oil interval.*

## Light PMS vs. Heavy PMS on the Hilux

Most Hilux visits are **light PMS** — diesel oil + filter and a general inspection, every 5,000 km. They cost more than a gas car's only because of the oil volume and the pricier low-ash diesel oil.

The **heavy (major) PMS** at **40,000 km and 80,000 km** is where the bill grows: the **fuel filter and water separator**, brake fluid, coolant, transmission and differential fluids (plus transfer-case and diff oil on the 4x4), the air-cleaner element, and a full diesel-system check. The 80,000 km service is the costliest, so budget for it ahead. Skimping on the fuel filter to save money is a false economy — it shields the expensive injectors and high-pressure pump. This matters even more on a Hilux that does farm, site, or provincial duty, where dirty fuel and dust are constant.

## Toyota Hilux: Timing Belt or Chain?

**Chain.** Both the 2.4L **2GD-FTV** and the 2.8L **1GD-FTV** diesel use a **timing chain**, not a rubber timing belt. That means there's **no scheduled timing-belt replacement** — none of the ₱15,000–30,000+ belt job that belt-driven engines need around 100,000 km. A chain is designed to last the engine's life on clean, on-time oil, which on a diesel matters even more: dirty or overdue oil is the fastest way to wear a chain and its tensioner early. Keep the DL-1 oil fresh and the chain looks after itself.

## Toyota Hilux DPF: What It Is and How to Keep It Healthy

Both Hilux diesels are fitted with a **DPF (Diesel Particulate Filter)** — a filter in the exhaust that traps soot and periodically burns it off ("regeneration") on longer, hotter drives. It's the single most misunderstood part of owning a modern diesel in the city, so it's worth getting right:

- **Pure slow city driving is the enemy.** If the engine never gets hot enough for long enough, the DPF can't regenerate and slowly clogs — eventually throwing a warning light. The fix is simple: give it a **regular highway or open-road run** (20–30 minutes at steady speed) every so often to let it clean itself.
- **Use only low-ash DL-1 diesel oil.** This isn't optional. The wrong (high-ash) oil ash-loads the DPF and shortens its life — one reason the casa uses DL-1 and you should insist a talyer does too.
- **Don't ignore the DPF warning.** If it comes on and a drive doesn't clear it, bring it in — a forced/manual regeneration at the casa is far cheaper than a clogged DPF that needs cleaning or replacement.
- **Keep the fuel clean.** Bad diesel and a neglected fuel filter make the DPF work harder; the two systems are linked — a real concern kung palaging nagf-fuel sa mga gasolinahan na kaduda-duda ang diesel.

Treat the DPF as a normal, manageable part of diesel ownership — not a flaw. Owners who mix in highway driving and stick to DL-1 oil rarely have trouble with it.

## Known Issues on the Toyota Hilux

Worth knowing, especially on the used market:

- **1GD-FTV low-speed hesitation / rough running (2016–2018).** Some early 2.8L units — the same 1GD family used across the Fortuner and Innova — showed a low-speed shudder or hesitation. Toyota addressed it with **ECU/calibration software updates** at the dealer; note this was a service/calibration fix (TSB-style), **not a formal safety recall**. On a pre-owned unit, test-drive at crawl speed and confirm the latest calibration was applied.
- **DPF clogging on pure city use.** The most common city-diesel complaint — the filter needs longer, hotter drives to regenerate, so pure slow city driving can clog it and trigger a warning. See the DPF care section above; the fix is a regular highway run plus low-ash DL-1 oil.
- **Injector and diesel-fuel sensitivity on hard-worked trucks.** A Hilux that lives on dusty routes or questionable diesel puts more stress on its fuel filter, injectors, and high-pressure pump than a city car does. This isn't a design fault — it's why the fuel filter and clean diesel matter so much on this truck specifically.
- **Heavier running costs than a gas vehicle** — expected for a diesel of this size; budget accordingly.

None of these dent the Hilux's reputation for toughness — they're checks, not dealbreakers.

## Toyota Hilux Warranty — and Why On-Time PMS Pays

Every new Hilux comes with Toyota Motor Philippines' standard **3-year / 100,000 km** manufacturer warranty. Since January 2025, Toyota PH has also offered a **Service Loyalty Warranty** that can stretch coverage to **up to 5 years / an extra 40,000 km** — *but only if you keep your PMS at a Toyota dealer* (at least one PMS per year through the warranty period). On a diesel pickup with expensive injectors, a high-pressure pump, and a DPF, that extended coverage is worth real money — and the on-time casa schedule during the warranty years is exactly what unlocks it. Keep every PMS receipt.

*(Standard warranty is 3 yr/100,000 km; the loyalty extension adds up to 2 yr/40,000 km for units sold January 1, 2025 onward, subject to Toyota's PMS conditions — confirm current terms with your dealer.)*

## Casa vs. Talyer & Parts

**Under warranty → Toyota casa** (keep every receipt). **Out of warranty → a trusted diesel-savvy talyer** is usually cheaper for the same work — basta tama ang diesel-spec (DL-1) oil at parts. Toyota's network is the densest in the country and the Hilux is everywhere, so parts and know-how are never a problem — one of the truck's biggest practical advantages over less common pickups. Between visits you can DIY the basics: tire pressure, washer fluid, cabin filter, and an oil-level eyeball; leave injector service, fuel-filter and DPF-related work, and anything warranty-related to the pros.

## Verdict: The Workhorse That Holds Its Value

If you need a tough, go-anywhere diesel pickup that earns its keep and holds its price, the Hilux is the benchmark — manageable diesel PMS for its size, a timing chain (no belt drama), parts on every corner, and resale among the strongest in its class. In 2026 the outgoing eighth-gen lineup spans roughly **₱891,000 (2.4 Cab & Chassis 4x2 MT)** to **₱2.46M (2.8 GR Sport 4x4 AT)**, even as the all-new ninth-gen and the Hilux BEV begin arriving *(Toyota PH SRP, July 2026 — verify with your dealer)*. Just respect the schedule, keep it on clean diesel and DL-1 oil, and keep your records clean.
