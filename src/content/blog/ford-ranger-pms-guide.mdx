The **Ford Ranger** is the workhorse sibling of the Everest — same T6 platform, same 2.0L EcoBlue diesel family, but built as a pickup that ranges from a bare-bones single-turbo XL to the 397-horsepower Raptor. It also carries the same "mahal daw magpa-maintain" reputation as its SUV twin, and the same open questions about long-term reliability of its wet-belt engine. This guide covers what's actually verified for the current Ranger — trim-by-trim engine and price breakdown, official PMS interval, recall history — and flags what's still unconfirmed rather than guessing. This is the Ranger companion to our [general PMS cost guide](/blog/car-pms-philippines-guide) and the [Ford Everest guide](/blog/ford-everest-pms-guide) for the SUV version of this same platform.

## Key Takeaways

- Current PH Ranger is the **3rd-generation "Next-Gen" model (T6/P703 platform)**, launched **July 28, 2022** — the same day as the Everest — with no structural facelift since, only running updates (bigger touchscreen on XLS/XLT, a new XL Plus variant).
- The lineup spans **from a manual-only XL 4x4 (2.0L Single-Turbo, 6-speed MT) up to the Raptor (3.0L V6 twin-turbo petrol, 397 PS/583 Nm, 10-speed AT)** — PH does **not** get the global diesel V6, only the petrol one on the Raptor.
- **Single-Turbo trims run a 6-speed transmission (MT or AT); Bi-Turbo and Raptor trims are 10-speed automatic only** — a genuine spec difference to check before you buy, not just a trim-name label.
- Like the Everest, both diesel engines use a **"wet" timing belt** (belt-in-oil), not a chain — the same long-term reliability question carries over.
- Ford PH's current PMS policy is **once a year: 12 months or 10,000 km**, whichever comes first — confirmed to apply identically to Ranger, Everest, and Territory.
- **One confirmed PH recall applies to the Ranger** (the Takata airbag campaign) — the 2023 E-Shifter recall that hit some Everest units does **not** cover the PH Ranger, per Ford PH's own clarification.

## Which Model Year Does This Guide Cover? (2022–2026)

This guide covers the **current, 3rd-generation "Next-Gen" Ranger (2022–present)** — whether you're looking at a 2022, 2024, or 2026 unit, it's the same T6/P703 platform, and Ford PH hasn't issued a structural facelift since launch. What has changed is trim detail: an **8-inch touchscreen** was added to the XLS and XLT trims partway through the run, and an **XL Plus** variant joined the lineup as a step-up from the base XL. If you're shopping used, the **previous-generation Ranger (2012–2022, with facelifts in 2015 and 2019)** is a completely different platform and engine lineup and is outside the scope of this guide — its maintenance history and known issues don't carry over. A diesel-V6 "Wildtrak-X" variant has been rumored as possibly PH-bound as of mid-2026 reporting, but it isn't part of the current lineup — don't shop for it yet.

## Engine & Transmission: Not Just a Trim-Name Difference

The Ranger's engine and transmission follow a clear split, and it's worth understanding before you shop by trim name alone. **Single-Turbo diesel trims** (XL through Wildtrak 4x2) get a **6-speed transmission**, available as either manual or automatic depending on variant — the **XL 4x4 MT is the only manual-transmission Ranger** in the current lineup, a genuine option for buyers who want a simpler, cheaper-to-maintain drivetrain. **Bi-Turbo diesel trims** (Wildtrak SE and 4x4 Wildtrak variants) step up to a more powerful ~207 hp/500 Nm tune paired exclusively with a **10-speed automatic** — no manual option once you're in Bi-Turbo territory. The **Raptor** sits apart entirely: it's the only Ranger sold in PH with the **3.0L V6 twin-turbo petrol EcoBoost** engine (the same unit used in some Mustang and F-150 variants globally), not a diesel, also paired with the 10-speed automatic. PH does **not** get the global 3.0L V6 turbo-diesel option offered in some other markets.

## PMS Interval: Once a Year, Like the Everest

Ford PH's aftersales program treats the Ranger identically to the Everest and Territory on the same platform: the **first PMS is due at 12 months or 10,000 km, whichever comes first; the second at 24 months or 20,000 km**. This applies across both the Single-Turbo and Bi-Turbo engines, and to manual and automatic transmissions alike. That's a genuine advantage over the twice-a-year (6-month) cadence most Japanese-brand rivals still run — fewer casa visits per year even at similar annual mileage. Ford PH has also run a **free-labor PMS promo** covering the first two services (12 mo/10,000 km and 24 mo/20,000 km) for units purchased from January 1, 2024 onward — worth confirming with your dealer whether your unit still qualifies.

## PMS Cost: What's Verified, What Isn't

As with the Everest, **Ford PH does not appear to publish a current, fixed PMS price list** for the Next-Gen Ranger — the brand uses an online Service Price Calculator instead of a static sheet, and its output couldn't be independently verified for this guide.

What we can confirm is historical context, and it's worth noting the Ranger had its own distinct figures separate from the Everest's. In the same April 2018 pricing controversy that hit the Everest, Ford PH cut Ranger maintenance costs too — reporting put the **Ranger's 5-year/100,000 km cost of ownership dropping from roughly ₱140,000 to about ₱120,000, a 15% decrease** (slightly less than the Everest's reported 18% cut, on the older 2.2L/3.2L engines of that era). A separate 2019 calculation for the then-current Ranger 2.0L EcoBlue Wildtrak 4x2+ put costs at roughly **₱1.10 per km, or about ₱110,260 total across 100,000 km**, with individual PMS visits ranging from about ₱4,770 to ₱18,950 depending on the service interval.

Those are 2018–2019-era figures, predating some of today's trims and possibly the Bi-Turbo/10-speed combination entirely, so **don't treat them as current pricing** for 2026 — but they're useful context for how the "Fords are mahal to maintain" reputation took hold, and a baseline to sanity-check whatever quote the Service Price Calculator gives you today.

## The Wet Timing Belt: Same Question as the Everest

The Ranger's 2.0L diesel engines — Single-Turbo and Bi-Turbo alike — share the Everest's **"wet" timing belt**: a belt-in-oil design instead of a conventional timing chain, meant to reduce friction. Ford's official change interval is **240,000 km or 10 years**, but this is also the engine family where most of the international "wet belt failure" discussion actually originates — owner and technician reports out of South Africa, the UK, and Australia describe some failures well short of the official interval, with preventive replacement sometimes recommended around 150,000–160,000 km as a precaution against belt debris clogging the oil pickup. PH ownership forums like Tsikot have their own threads raising the same concern, though detailed local failure/km figures are harder to pin down than the international reports. If engine longevity over a long ownership period matters to you — especially if you're buying for towing or heavy-load use — ask your dealer directly about this design and whether any running changes have addressed it on your specific build.

## Known Issues on the Ford Ranger

- **Wet timing belt** (see above) — the same long-term reliability question as the Everest, and arguably more consequential here given how many Rangers see towing or payload duty.
- **10-speed automatic transmission (Bi-Turbo and Raptor trims).** PH owner discussion (via Philkotse) includes reports from Filipino Ranger and Raptor owners describing stalling behavior with the 10-speed AT, serious enough that a possible class-action conversation against Ford PH has come up in that reporting. This is a genuine PH-sourced concern, though it isn't yet tied to a specific published technical service bulletin for this generation — treat it as a real, worth-asking-about issue rather than a confirmed defect with a known fix.
- **DPF/EGR under load.** As a diesel built for towing and payload work, short-trip or low-load city driving can prevent the DPF from regenerating properly — a general modern-diesel caveat that matters more on a Ranger than on a passenger car, since so many are bought specifically for hauling. Give it regular longer/highway runs and use the correct low-ash diesel oil.
- **Unladen ride quality.** Like most leaf-sprung pickups, the Ranger rides stiffer and bouncier when the bed is empty than when it's loaded — a known design tradeoff of the segment, not a defect, but worth test-driving unladen if that's how you'll mostly use it.
- **Raptor-specific notes.** PH owner reviews of the Raptor skew largely positive, with the FOX suspension's firmer, sportier ride occasionally drawing comment from buyers expecting a softer daily-driver feel — again a design characteristic, not a fault.

## Recall History: One Confirmed PH Campaign, One Explicitly Excluded

- **Takata airbag inflators (2022, relaunched 2026).** This is the recall that does apply — around 32,900 PH vehicles across the Everest, Ranger, and Mustang lineups were covered, announced June 2022 and relaunched by Ford PH in January 2026 for owners who hadn't yet completed the repair. If you're shopping a used Ranger, checking recall status by VIN is worth doing.
- **E-Shifter unexpected Park engagement (2023) — does NOT cover the PH Ranger.** This software fault affected certain 2021–2023 Everest Titanium 4x4 units globally, but Ford PH itself confirmed that in the Philippines, only a small number of Everest units were affected and **the Ranger was not part of this recall locally**. Worth knowing since some general Ford recall summaries lump Everest and Ranger together without this distinction.
- **10-speed transmission fluid pump gear (2020).** This earlier recall covered Thailand-built Ranger and Raptor units with the previous-generation 2.0L powertrain, alongside Everest Titanium units of the same era — it's tied to the prior generation, not confirmed to extend to the current Next-Gen (2022+) Ranger's 10-speed unit.

No PH-specific pickup recall (bed, frame, tailgate, driveshaft) was found for the current generation. Always confirm your specific VIN with a Ford dealer, since coverage is unit-specific and recall status can change as campaigns are relaunched.

## Dealer Network & Casa vs. Talyer

The Ranger is sold and serviced through the same **Ford Group Philippines, Inc.** network as the Everest — there's no separate commercial-vehicle distributor for the pickup. That means the same roughly **50 dealerships nationwide** (2024–2025) apply, smaller than Toyota's or Mitsubishi's network but a genuine factory-owned operation with its own parts distribution center in Calamba, Laguna.

**Under warranty → Ford casa**, to protect your coverage and because the wet-belt engine and multi-speed transmissions are specialized enough to want factory diagnostic tools. **Out of warranty → a trusted diesel/pickup-savvy talyer** can save real money on routine work, but leave the timing belt, DPF service, and transmission work — especially on Bi-Turbo/10-speed units — to technicians who specifically know this engine family.

## What You Can DIY

Tire pressure (especially important on a pickup that alternates between loaded and unloaded use — pressures often differ), washer fluid, cabin filter swaps, and wiper blades are fine to handle yourself between visits. Leave the timing-belt system, DPF-related service, and any transmission work to Ford-trained technicians, particularly if you're running the Bi-Turbo or Raptor.

## Verdict: A Capable Workhorse With the Same Homework as the Everest

The current Ranger is a well-reviewed pickup — flexible enough to span a bare manual XL work truck up to a genuinely quick Raptor — and its once-a-year PMS interval is a real, documented advantage that carries over from its Everest sibling. The trade-offs carry over too: no confirmed current PMS pricing to budget against, a wet timing belt whose long-term durability is still debated (arguably more relevant here given how many Rangers tow or haul), and PH-sourced owner concerns about the 10-speed automatic worth asking your dealer about directly. Confirm your trim's exact engine and transmission combination, get a Service Price Calculator quote, and check the VIN against the Takata recall before you sign.
