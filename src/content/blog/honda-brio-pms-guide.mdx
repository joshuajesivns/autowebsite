The **Honda Brio** is the fun-to-drive budget hatchback — a peppy 1.2L i-VTEC, light body, and Honda's reputation for engines that love to rev. It's still sold new in the Philippines in 2026 (2nd-generation, facelifted in 2023), imported complete from Honda's Karawang, Indonesia plant. It's cheap to keep running — but the PMS interval most owners assume is wrong. Here's exactly what a Brio costs to maintain, light PMS versus heavy PMS, and what's actually verified. This is the Brio companion to our [Philippine car PMS cost guide](/blog/car-pms-philippines-guide).

## Key Takeaways

- **Key correction:** the Brio's PMS interval is **10,000 km / 6 months** — Honda Philippines' brand-wide standard (same as the City), not the 5,000 km cycle Toyota/Mitsubishi models run.
- Honda PH has an official **PM Cost Calculator**; ballpark a light PMS at **₱3,000–5,500** and a heavy/major PMS higher — get the exact figure for your variant there.
- The lineup is **mixed**: base **S** is 5-speed manual; **V, RS, RS Black Top** get a CVT.
- The **1.2L L12B i-VTEC** (~89 hp/110 Nm) is frugal and reliable; just feed it on schedule.
- **Timing chain, not belt** = walang mahal na belt replacement later.
- **No PH recall** on record for the Brio.

## Which Model Year Does This Guide Cover? (2019–2026)

This guide covers the **2nd-generation Honda Brio**, on sale in the Philippines since **April 2019**, including the **2023 facelift** that remains the current model through 2026. That facelift didn't just refresh the styling — it also split the transmission by trim: base **S** is 5-speed manual only, while **V, RS, and RS Black Top** all get a CVT. Whether your Brio is a pre-facelift 2019–2022 unit or a post-facelift 2023–2026 unit, it's the same 2nd-generation platform and engine, so the PMS interval and known issues below apply across the board — just confirm which transmission your specific trim has.

## Honda Brio PMS Cost by Interval

The **most common mistake owners make is servicing the Brio every 5,000 km**, assuming it follows the Toyota/Mitsubishi rhythm. Honda Philippines' official policy — stated across its own service materials for the Brio-to-Legend lineup — is **10,000 km or 6 months**. Stretching beyond that in PH heat and traffic ("severe" driving) isn't advisable, but there's no need to double your visits either.

Honda PH runs an official **PM Cost Calculator** by model and mileage, so treat the figures below as a ballpark, not a quote:

| PMS stage | Cost (Brio, estimate) |
|---|---|
| First PMS | low / often free labor |
| Light / standard PMS | ₱3,000–5,500 |
| Heavy / major PMS | higher (more parts + fluids replaced) |

For the full interval-by-interval breakdown of *what's actually done* at each stop, see the [full breakdown of PMS intervals and prices](/blog/car-pms-philippines-guide).

## Light PMS vs. Heavy PMS on the Brio

Most 10,000 km stops are **light PMS** — engine oil + filter, fluid top-ups, and a general inspection. The **heavy (major) PMS**, usually every second or third visit, adds air and fuel filter replacement, spark plugs, brake fluid, and — on CVT variants — a **CVT fluid change**. Confirm the exact menu and CVT-fluid interval for your specific variant with your Honda dealer; no verified official figure for that interval was available at the time of writing.

## Known Issues on the Honda Brio

Reliable and simple overall, with genuinely high owner satisfaction — pero may mga bagay na dapat malaman bago bumili o habang nagmamay-ari:

- **Sparse safety kit on lower trims.** No ISOFIX/LATCH rear child-seat anchors, and no front sensors, camera, cruise control, or Honda Sensing on any Brio variant. Budget-segment trade-off, not a defect — but worth knowing if safety tech is a priority.
- **Braking feel.** A recurring owner/reviewer comment is that the brakes feel soft or need firmer pedal pressure than expected; still stops the car fine, just a known character trait to get used to.
- **Ride on sharp bumps.** Handles big road imperfections decently, but can feel harsh over sharp, small bumps — a byproduct of the light body and budget suspension tuning.
- **Engine mounts** — Hondas rev happily, and mounts can soften over the years; a source of vibration.
- **Pang-ilalim** — being a light car it rides well, but bushings and links still wear on PH roads. Listen for `lagutok` and `kalog` — we cover diagnosing those suspension noises in our [Philippine car servicing explainer](/blog/car-pms-philippines-guide).
- **Aircon** — PH heat works the system hard; expect a clean/recharge as it ages.

No Philippine recall is currently listed for the Brio on Honda PH's own recall page (its active campaigns name the Civic, Accord, CR-V, City, Jazz, Pilot, BR-V, Amaze, and WR-V — not the Brio).

## Casa vs. Talyer for a Brio

**Under warranty → Honda casa** (keep your receipts). **Out of warranty → a trusted talyer** is usually cheaper for the same work, basta tama ang oil at parts spec.

Honda's network and parts availability are good — not quite as ubiquitous as Toyota's, but easy enough in most areas, at abot-kaya ang parts ng Brio.

## What You Can DIY

Between visits: check tire pressure, top up washer fluid, swap the cabin filter, and eyeball your oil level. Leave CVT-fluid work, brake work, and warranty service to the pros.

## Verdict: The Fun Budget Hatch That's Cheap to Keep — Just Service It Right

If you want a little driving joy without giving up low running costs, the Brio still delivers — modest PMS bills, a frugal i-VTEC, and a timing chain (no belt drama). The one thing to fix in your head: it's a **10,000 km car, not a 5,000 km car**. Get that right and keep your records clean.
