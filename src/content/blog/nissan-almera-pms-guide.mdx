The **Nissan Almera** is the value play in the subcompact sedan class — roomy, well-equipped, and (in its current form) running a punchy little **1.0L turbo**. Maintaining it is affordable, but it has one quirk most owners miss: its PMS interval is **7,000 km**, not the 5,000 km of most rivals or even Nissan's own 10,000 km default. Here's exactly what an Almera costs to service, that unusual schedule explained, plus the honest truth about its CVT. This is the Almera companion to our [complete car PMS and pricing guide](/blog/car-pms-philippines-guide).

## Key Takeaways

- The Almera runs an unusual **7,000 km / 6-month** PMS cycle — an official Nissan PH exception (their default is 10,000 km).
- A regular Almera PMS is roughly **₱4,000–6,000**; the first PMS is ~₱2,000. *(Rough casa estimates, June 2026 — Nissan PH doesn't publish a clean Almera price list, so verify with your dealer.)*
- **Turbo discipline (1.0L):** use the correct-spec oil and never stretch the interval — small turbos are less forgiving of dirty oil.
- **The CVT is fine** — it's a lighter-duty unit, not the failure-prone CVT8 from big US Nissans. Keep the fluid fresh.
- **Know your generation:** current N18 (2021+) = 1.0L turbo + CVT/manual; old N17 (~2012–2021) = 1.5L naturally-aspirated + 4-speed auto.
- **Timing chain, not belt** = walang mahal na belt replacement later.

## Which Model Year Does This Guide Cover? (October 2021–2026)

This guide covers the current **N18-generation Almera**, launched in the Philippines in October 2021 — the 1.0L HR10DET turbo with a 5-speed manual or Xtronic CVT. The older **N17 generation (~2012–2021, locally assembled in Sta. Rosa)** is a different car under the skin: a 1.5L HR15DE naturally-aspirated engine with a 4-speed automatic, no turbo and no CVT. Its PMS schedule, oil spec, and known issues aren't the same as what's covered here.

## The Almera's Unusual 7,000 km Interval

This is the one thing to get right. Most cars in the Philippines are serviced every 5,000 km (Toyota, Mitsubishi) or 10,000 km. The Almera is **neither** — Nissan Philippines' own maintenance page lists the general Nissan interval as 10,000 km **with a specific footnote of 7,000 km for the Almera**.

In practice that means your service stops fall at **1,000 km** (break-in), then every **7,000 km** — 7k, 14k, 21k, 28k, and so on. Don't book it on the 5,000 km habit (you'll over-service and overspend) or stretch it to 10,000 km (you'll under-service the turbo). Set your reminder to 7,000 km or 6 months, whichever comes first.

The turbo doesn't make routine PMS dramatically pricier; it just raises the stakes on doing it *on time* with the right oil. ## Light PMS vs. Heavy PMS on the Almera

Most visits are **light PMS** — engine oil + filter, tire rotation, and a general check, every 7,000 km. The **heavy (major) PMS** is when the casa also replaces the occasional-wear items: air and fuel filters, brake fluid, the **CVT fluid (Nissan NS-3)**, and spark plugs. Because the Almera counts in 7,000 km steps, these bigger services don't land on the usual "40k/80k" marks — they come due around the higher-mileage stops, so confirm the exact schedule with your dealer rather than assuming the standard milestones.

## Known Issues on the Nissan Almera

A solid car, pero may babantayan — especially around the turbo and CVT:

- **The Nissan CVT reputation — and the reality.** Nissan's CVT horror stories (judder, overheating, early failure) are concentrated in the **heavier-duty CVT8** used in big US models like the Altima and Rogue. The Almera uses a **lighter-duty CVT** behind a low-torque 100 PS engine and **hasn't shown that failure pattern**. The practical care: keep the CVT fluid fresh (Nissan NS-3 only) and avoid letting it overheat in long heavy traffic — an honest precaution, not a sign of a known flaw.
- **Slightly jerky low-speed CVT feel (pre-2024 units).** Some earlier units had a noticeable judder in stop-and-go traffic; Nissan calibrated this out on newer model years. Characteristic, not a fault.
- **Turbo oil discipline.** The 1.0L turbo has **no corroborated oil-burning or turbo defect** — but as with any small turbo, correct-spec oil changed on time is the single best habit for longevity. (Ignore the old "dirty oil kills the Almera's chain" stories online — those are about 2000s-era Almeras, a completely different engine.)
- **Three-cylinder vibration at idle.** A light shake at stoplights is inherent to a 1.0L triple; it's well-damped by the mounts but you'll feel a little.
- **Cabin noise (NVH).** Thin insulation means road and tire noise climb from around 70 km/h — typical for the value class.
- **Pang-ilalim wear.** Bushings, tie-rod ends, and stabilizer links take a beating on PH roads — listen for `lagutok` and `kalog`. Full sound guide in our [companion write-up on car maintenance in PH](/blog/car-pms-philippines-guide).

Good news on the safety front: there is **no Philippine recall on record for the Almera** (the Nissan PH Takata airbag recall did not include it).

## Casa vs. Talyer & Parts

**Under warranty → Nissan casa** (keep your receipts). **Out of warranty → a trusted talyer** is usually cheaper — but for the turbo, make sure the shop uses the correct oil spec, and for the CVT, the correct NS-3 fluid. One honest note: Nissan's dealer network and parts availability aren't quite as dense as Toyota's, so plan a little ahead for parts on the turbo model. Between visits you can DIY the basics — tire pressure, washer fluid, cabin filter, oil-level check — and leave CVT fluid, turbo work, and warranty service to the pros.

## Verdict: Great Value If You Respect the Schedule

The Almera gives you more space and a peppier turbo for the money than most rivals, with affordable routine PMS and a timing chain (no belt drama). It's **still sold new in 2026** (1.0 EL manual, VE and VL CVT). The deal: book it on its real **7,000 km** interval, treat oil and CVT-fluid changes as non-negotiable, and keep your records clean — do that and it's an easy, dependable car to live with.
