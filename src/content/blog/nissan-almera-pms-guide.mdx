The **Nissan Almera** is the value play in the subcompact sedan class — roomy, well-equipped, and (in its current form) running a punchy little **1.0L turbo**. Maintaining it is affordable, but it has one quirk most owners miss: its PMS interval is **7,000 km**, not the 5,000 km of most rivals or even Nissan's own 10,000 km default. Here's exactly what an Almera costs to service, that unusual schedule explained, plus the honest truth about its CVT. This is the Almera companion to our [complete car PMS and pricing guide](/blog/car-pms-philippines-guide).

## Key Takeaways

- The Almera runs an unusual **7,000 km / 6-month** PMS cycle — an official Nissan PH exception (their default is 10,000 km).
- A regular Almera PMS is roughly **₱4,000–6,000**; the first PMS is ~₱2,000. *(Rough casa estimates, June 2026 — Nissan PH doesn't publish a clean Almera price list, so verify with your dealer.)*
- **Turbo discipline (1.0L):** use the correct-spec oil and never stretch the interval — small turbos are less forgiving of dirty oil.
- **The CVT is fine** — it's a lighter-duty unit, not the failure-prone CVT8 from big US Nissans. Keep the fluid fresh.
- **Know your generation:** current N18 (2021+) = 1.0L turbo + CVT/manual; old N17 (~2012–2021) = 1.5L naturally-aspirated + 4-speed auto.
- **The car on sale now is the 2024 facelift** — the N-Sport trim was dropped, VL is the top variant, and **6 airbags are now standard on all three variants** (EL, VE, VL).
- **Timing chain, not belt** = walang mahal na belt replacement later.
- **Current 2026 SRP:** EL (MT) ~₱845,000, VE (CVT) ~₱1,099,000, VL (CVT) ~₱1,199,000. *(Nissan PH price guide, July 2026 — confirm with your dealer.)*

## Which Model Year Does This Guide Cover? (October 2021–2026)

This guide covers the current **N18-generation Almera**, launched in the Philippines in October 2021 — the 1.0L turbo with a 5-speed manual or Xtronic CVT. Importantly, the car on sale today is the **2024 facelift**: it kept the same HRA0/HR10DET turbo engine and 7,000 km schedule, but the lineup was reshuffled (the sportier **N-Sport** trim was dropped, **VL** became the top variant), NissanConnect Services was added on the CVT trims, and — a real upgrade — **6 airbags became standard across all three variants (EL, VE, VL)**, where the pre-facelift base only had two. Nothing in the maintenance picture changed with the facelift, so everything below applies to both the 2021–2023 pre-facelift and 2024–2026 facelift cars.

The older **N17 generation (~2012–2021, locally assembled in Sta. Rosa until CKD production wound down in 2020)** is a different car under the skin: a 1.5L HR15DE naturally-aspirated engine with a 4-speed automatic, no turbo and no CVT. Its PMS schedule, oil spec, and known issues aren't the same as what's covered here.

## The Almera's Unusual 7,000 km Interval

This is the one thing to get right. Most cars in the Philippines are serviced every 5,000 km (Toyota, Mitsubishi) or 10,000 km. The Almera is **neither** — Nissan Philippines' own maintenance page lists the general Nissan interval as 10,000 km **with a specific footnote of 7,000 km for the Almera**.

In practice that means your service stops fall at **1,000 km** (break-in), then every **7,000 km** — 7k, 14k, 21k, 28k, and so on. Don't book it on the 5,000 km habit (you'll over-service and overspend) or stretch it to 10,000 km (you'll under-service the turbo). Set your reminder to 7,000 km or 6 months, whichever comes first.

## Light PMS vs. Heavy PMS on the Almera

Most visits are **light PMS** — engine oil + filter, tire rotation, and a general check, every 7,000 km. On the 1.0L turbo this is the single most important habit for longevity: correct-spec oil, changed on schedule. The **heavy (major) PMS** is when the casa also replaces the occasional-wear items: air and fuel filters, brake fluid, the **CVT fluid (Nissan NS-3)**, and spark plugs. Because the Almera counts in 7,000 km steps, these bigger services don't land on the usual "40k/80k" marks — they come due around the higher-mileage stops, so confirm the exact schedule with your dealer rather than assuming the standard milestones.

## Nissan Almera: Timing Belt or Chain?

**Chain.** The Almera's 1.0L turbo (the HR engine family) uses a **timing chain**, not a rubber timing belt. That's good news for running costs: there's **no scheduled timing-belt replacement** — the ₱15,000–30,000+ job that belt-driven cars need around 100,000 km simply doesn't exist here. A chain is designed to last the engine's life *as long as the oil stays clean*, which is one more reason oil discipline matters on the turbo — the chain and the turbo both rely on it. And to be clear: the scary "Almera timing-chain failure" stories you'll find online are about **2000s-era N16 Almeras with a completely different engine** — not this one.

## Nissan Almera CVT Fluid Change Interval

The VE and VL use Nissan's **Xtronic CVT**, which takes a specific fluid — **Nissan NS-3**, never generic ATF (wrong fluid is an immediate damage risk, so this one belongs at the casa). Here's the honest part: **Nissan PH doesn't publish a fixed km interval for the Almera's CVT fluid.** In practice it's inspected and replaced around a major-service stop (roughly every 40,000–60,000 km, sooner if you're always in severe heavy-traffic driving), but treat that as owner/casa practice, not an official figure — confirm the exact interval for your car with your dealer. The base **EL is a 5-speed manual**, so it has no CVT fluid to worry about (just regular manual-transmission oil).

## Known Issues on the Nissan Almera

A solid car, pero may babantayan — especially around the turbo and CVT:

- **The Nissan CVT reputation — and the reality.** Nissan's CVT horror stories (judder, overheating, early failure) are concentrated in the **heavier-duty CVT8** used in big US models like the Altima and Rogue. The Almera uses a **lighter-duty CVT** behind a low-torque 100 PS engine and **hasn't shown that failure pattern**. The practical care: keep the CVT fluid fresh (Nissan NS-3 only) and avoid letting it overheat in long heavy traffic — an honest precaution, not a sign of a known flaw.
- **Slightly jerky low-speed CVT feel (pre-2024 units).** Some earlier units had a noticeable judder in stop-and-go traffic; Nissan calibrated this out on newer model years. Characteristic, not a fault.
- **Turbo oil discipline.** The 1.0L turbo has **no corroborated oil-burning or turbo defect** — but as with any small turbo, correct-spec oil changed on time is the single best habit for longevity. (Ignore the old "dirty oil kills the Almera's chain" stories online — those are about 2000s-era Almeras, a completely different engine.)
- **Three-cylinder vibration at idle.** A light shake at stoplights is inherent to a 1.0L triple; it's well-damped by the mounts but you'll feel a little.
- **Cabin noise (NVH).** Thin insulation means road and tire noise climb from around 70 km/h — typical for the value class.
- **Pang-ilalim wear.** Bushings, tie-rod ends, and stabilizer links take a beating on PH roads — listen for `lagutok` and `kalog`. Full sound guide in our [companion write-up on car maintenance in PH](/blog/car-pms-philippines-guide).

Good news on the safety front: there is **no Philippine recall on record for the Almera** (the Nissan PH Takata airbag recall did not include it).

## Casa vs. Talyer & Parts

**Under warranty → Nissan casa** (keep your receipts). **Out of warranty → a trusted talyer** is usually cheaper — but for the turbo, make sure the shop uses the correct oil spec, and for the CVT, the correct NS-3 fluid. One honest note: Nissan's dealer network and parts availability aren't quite as dense as Toyota's, so plan a little ahead for parts on the turbo model. Between visits you can DIY the basics — tire pressure, washer fluid, cabin filter, oil-level check — and leave CVT fluid, turbo work, and warranty service to the pros.

If the unpredictable PMS bill bothers you, Nissan PH offers a prepaid, fixed-price **Service Protection Plan** — you lock in labor and parts pricing for a set number of years/services upfront. It's worth asking your dealer to quote it against pay-as-you-go: it removes the "magkano kaya this time?" guesswork that comes from Nissan not publishing an Almera price list, though whether it saves money depends on your mileage.

## Verdict: Great Value If You Respect the Schedule

The Almera gives you more space and a peppier turbo for the money than most rivals, with affordable routine PMS and a timing chain (no belt drama). It's **still sold new in 2026** — the 2024-facelift lineup is the EL (MT, ~₱845,000), VE (CVT, ~₱1,099,000), and VL (CVT, ~₱1,199,000), now with 6 airbags standard across the board *(Nissan PH SRP, July 2026 — verify with your dealer)*. The deal: book it on its real **7,000 km** interval, treat oil and CVT-fluid changes as non-negotiable, and keep your records clean — do that and it's an easy, dependable car to live with.
