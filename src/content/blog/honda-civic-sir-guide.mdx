The **Honda Civic SiR** is one of the most romanticized cars in Philippine enthusiast history — the affordable, high-revving VTEC sedan that turned a whole generation of local drivers into Honda people. Sold here by **Honda Cars Philippines** starting in **1998**, it took the humble EK Civic sedan body and dropped in the **B16A2** — a 1.6-liter DOHC VTEC that spins to past 8,000 rpm and makes **160 PS**. Here's what it actually is, what it becomes when you build it, and — most importantly for the PH market — how to tell a real one from a convincing fake.

<Pullquote>Hindi 'to sasakyan lang, project 'to.</Pullquote>

## Key Takeaways

- The PH-market SiR is the **EK4-chassis, 4-door sedan** offered by Honda Cars Philippines from **1998 through 2000** — same body shell as the VTi/LXi, but a different animal under the hood.
- Its engine is the **B16A2**: 1.6L DOHC VTEC, **~160 PS @ 7,600 rpm**, ~150 Nm, VTEC crossover around **5,600 rpm**, redline near **8,200 rpm**. From the factory it also carried an **oil cooler, knock sensor, four-wheel disc brakes, and a front strut bar**.
- This is **not** the EK9 Civic Type R — that's the higher-strung **B16B** (~185 PS), never officially sold here. Don't pay Type R money for a SiR.
- The **single biggest PH buying risk** is a **VTi/LXi converted to look like a SiR** — badge, body kit, sometimes a full B16 swap. Verifying the engine against the papers and the body's originality matters more than any test drive.
- Parts and B-series knowledge are **abundant in the Philippines** — Banawe/Evangelista surplus, a deep B16/B18 swap culture, and active owner communities make this a far easier car to keep running than most '90s JDM icons.
- As a modern buy, the real risk is **age and history** (abuse, over-rev, sketchy "refreshes"), not the engine's design — the B16A2 is a genuinely durable unit when maintained.

## What Is the Civic SiR, and Why Does It Matter?

The SiR is the performance trim of Honda's **sixth-generation Civic** (the EK generation, roughly 1995–2000), which enthusiasts tag by chassis code as the **EK4** — the B16-powered version. In the Philippines, Honda Cars Philippines slotted it above the everyday **VTi** and **LXi** sedans: same clean three-box EK body, but with the good engine, better brakes, and a sportier chassis setup.

What made it a landmark here wasn't styling — it's a subtle-looking sedan — it was the powertrain. The **B16A2** is a genuinely high-revving, naturally-aspirated VTEC that comes alive past 5,600 rpm and pulls hard toward an 8,000-plus-rpm redline, all while sending power to the front wheels through a slick 5-speed manual. In an era when local performance meant big, heavy, and thirsty, the SiR was light, revvy, and — crucially — attainable. It's widely credited with kicking off the Philippine VTEC/tuner scene, and it sat alongside the arrival of cars like the Nissan 200SX and Mitsubishi Eclipse as the country's affordable-performance moment.

That's also why it's so heavily cloned today — which we'll get to.

*A quick note on the engine code: the PH/export SiR uses the **B16A2**. The often-quoted plain **"B16A"** is the JDM SiR/SiR-II engine — same family, slightly different tune. And the **B16B** is a different, higher-output engine specific to the EK9 Type R, not the SiR.*

## Model Year Coverage

This guide covers the **EK4-chassis Honda Civic SiR sedan as sold by Honda Cars Philippines, 1998–2000**. The broader EK Civic generation ran globally from about 1995 to 2000 and included hatch and coupe bodies in other markets, plus the separate EK9 Type R — none of those were the official PH SiR. If you're looking at a hatch, a coupe, or a Type R locally, you're looking at an import or a conversion, which is a different due-diligence conversation than the one below.

## Common Upgrade Paths & Popular Kits

The SiR sits on the **B-series** platform — arguably the most modification-friendly Honda engine family ever, with a mature parts ecosystem. Most of what follows is well-established community practice; treat specific power figures as ballpark, not guarantees.

- **Bolt-ons first:** a proper 4-2-1 header, cat-back exhaust, cold-air intake, and an ECU tune (or chipped OBD ECU on these older cars) are the classic first steps. Modest gains, but they wake up the top end where the B16 already wants to live.
- **Breathing + valvetrain:** intake manifold, throttle body, and cam upgrades are common next moves. If you cam it and chase the rev range hard, upgraded valve springs/retainers are worth it — the recurring failure people report on abused B16s is **valvetrain trouble (dropped valves) from repeated over-rev**, not bottom-end grenading.
- **The deeper N/A build:** many owners go the full B-series route — B18C/Type R head-swap territory, higher compression, individual throttle bodies. This is where the platform's swap culture really pays off locally.
- **Forced induction:** turbo and supercharger kits exist, but the honest limit is that the **stock internals and head** get unhappy as boost and power climb — enthusiasts widely cite trouble (head lift, part fatigue) once you push well past stock power on the OEM long-block. Boosted SiRs need built internals and fueling to be reliable, not just a bolt-on kit.
- **Chassis:** coilovers, sway bars, and stiffer bushings are cheap and plentiful for the EK. An LSD is the high-value handling mod to actually put the power down through the front wheels.

## Parts Availability & Sourcing in PH

Here's where the SiR has a huge advantage over rarer JDM icons: **the Philippines is B-series country.** Between **Manila's Banawe and Evangelista surplus-parts districts**, a decades-deep B16/B18 swap scene, and countless shops that know these engines cold, keeping a SiR alive is genuinely easier than sourcing for, say, [the underrated AE92 Corolla](/blog/toyota-corolla-ae92-guide) or anything RWD-Toyota.

- **Common wear and service parts** (gaskets, bushings, brakes, cooling, clutch) are readily available — a lot of it interchanges across the EG/EK B-series family.
- **Whole engines and half-cuts** show up regularly in the surplus channel, which is exactly *why* conversions are so common (a B16 isn't hard to find).
- **SiR-specific trim and interior bits** (correct badges, gauges, seats) are the harder part — because they're what people strip and swap onto clones, genuine untouched examples get picked over.
- **Grey-market reality:** because the swap culture is so mature, "matching numbers" originality is rarer than you'd think. That's not automatically bad for a project car — but it changes what you should pay, and it's central to the buying section next.

## Known Weak Points — Modified / Track Use

Different risk profile from a stock daily. If you're building or tracking a SiR, these are the recurring themes:

- **Valvetrain over-rev damage.** The most-cited hard failure on abused B16s is dropping a valve from repeated over-rev (missed shifts, no rev-limiter discipline, tired springs). Upgraded springs/retainers and honest shifting matter more than headline power.
- **OEM long-block limits under boost.** The stock head and internals are the ceiling — pushing well beyond stock power on forced induction invites head lift and part fatigue. Plan for built internals *before* the turbo, not after it fails.
- **Heat management.** These engines live at high rpm; the factory oil cooler helps, but hard track use rewards a good radiator, oil cooler, and fresh cooling system. Old rubber and clogged cores are the real-world failure, not the design.
- **High-mileage bottom-end noise.** Rod knock on a 25-year-old, hard-driven unit is an age-and-history problem — it's *not* a documented endemic flaw of the B16A2 specifically. Judge the individual car's history, not the model.
- **Brakes and chassis for track duty.** The stock 4-wheel discs are good for a '90s sedan but modest by modern standards — pads, fluid, and bushings are the sensible upgrades before chasing more power.

## Buying Guide — The PH "Real vs. Replica" Problem

Forget importing: the SiR was **locally sold**, so almost every one you'll see is an in-country used car. The defining issue isn't rust or paperwork exotica — it's **authenticity**. Because a B16 swap is easy and the SiR badge carries a real premium, **VTi and LXi sedans get converted into "SiRs"** all the time. Some are honest project cars; some are priced as the genuine article. Protect yourself:

- **Match the engine to the papers.** A real SiR's B16 should be documented as its original engine on the OR/CR — not a later swap logged onto a car that left the factory with a SOHC economy engine. A mismatch here is the single clearest tell.
- **Verify the body is original, not converted.** Genuine listings often say so explicitly (owners advertise an *original SiR body, not converted* precisely because buyers ask). Look for consistency in the shell, VIN/chassis stamping, and factory SiR-specific details rather than just a bolted-on kit and badges.
- **Prefer closer-to-stock examples.** The most convincing replicas are almost always heavily modified — modification hides origins. A cleaner, more stock car is easier to authenticate, full stop.
- **Use the community.** There's an active **Philippine Civic SiR owner/registry community** — tapping it before you pay is the cheapest insurance you'll ever buy on one of these.
- **Then do the normal 25-year-old-car checks:** compression/leak-down on the B16, clutch and synchro health on the 5-speed, rust in the usual sedan spots, and an honest read on how hard the previous owner lived in VTEC.

To be clear, a converted VTi with a clean B16 swap can be a perfectly good car — the problem is only when it's *sold as* a factory SiR at factory-SiR money. Know which one you're buying.

## Resale & Collector Trajectory — Set Expectations Honestly

The SiR occupies an unusual spot. It has deep nostalgic value in the Philippines — for a lot of enthusiasts it's *the* first-love JDM car — and clean, genuine, low-modification examples clearly command a premium over an ordinary EK sedan. But because so many cars in the market are converted or heavily modified, there's **no clean "market average" to quote**, and a badge-driven premium invites exactly the kind of clone that muddies pricing. We'd rather not print a number that a seller could wave around out of context.

What we'll say with confidence: value tracks **authenticity and originality**, not just the SiR name. A verified, documented, close-to-stock car is the one with a real appreciation case. A tarted-up VTi is worth what a nice modified EG/EK is worth — which can still be a fun, fair buy, just not a collector one. Buy the specific car, not the legend.

## Verdict: The Car That Built PH VTEC Culture

The Civic SiR earns its legend honestly: a light, revvy, 160-PS sedan on the most tuner-friendly Honda platform ever made, backed by the deepest parts and knowledge base of any '90s JDM car in the country. That accessibility is also its curse — it's the most-cloned performance car on the local used market. So the whole game is authentication: verify the engine against the papers, confirm the body's real, lean on the owner community, and pay for the car in front of you rather than the badge on the trunk. Do that, and the SiR is still one of the most rewarding ways into JDM ownership in the Philippines.
