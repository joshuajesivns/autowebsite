The sixth-generation **Toyota Celica** — chassis code **T200**, the one with the rounded quad headlights — is the last Celica that still mattered to hardcore enthusiasts, because it's the last one you could get as the rally-bred **GT-Four (ST205)**: 2.0-liter turbo, full-time AWD, and a direct relative of Toyota's World Rally Championship car. Around that halo sits a whole range — humble FWD 1.8s, the sweet JDM 3S-GE **SS** trims, even a US 2.2 GT and a convertible. This guide is for the PH enthusiast eyeing one: what the T200 actually is across its trims, what the GT-Four becomes when you build it, and — most importantly here — what to check before you buy an import that's now 25-plus years old.

## Key Takeaways

- The **T200** ran from **late 1993 (Japan) / 1994 model year (worldwide) through 1999**. It's the generation with **round headlights** and the **only** Celica sold with the **GT-Four turbo AWD** — later T230 Celicas are FWD-only.
- The **GT-Four (ST205)** is the flagship: **2.0L 3S-GTE turbo**, full-time AWD, **~255 PS JDM / ~242 PS export**, manual-only. Roughly **2,500 were built** (a WRC homologation special), and clean examples now command real money globally.
- Below it: **FWD** ST/GT on **7A-FE (1.8L, ~105 hp)** or the **US-only 5S-FE (2.2L, 135 hp)**, and the JDM **SS-I/SS-II/SS-III** on the **2.0L 3S-GE** (~180 PS, rising to **200 PS** once the **BEAMS VVT-i** engine arrived in late 1997).
- In the Philippines this is a **used / JDM-import car** — we found no evidence Toyota PH ever sold it new. Treat it as an import-grade project, not a casa-backed buy.
- The **biggest real-world enemy is rust** (floor pans, strut towers, rockers) — brutal on a humid, occasionally-flooded market — followed on the GT-Four by **head-gasket-after-overheat** and the **expensive, wear-prone Super Strut** front suspension.
- **Engines are the easy part to source** (the 3S family is shared with the MR2, Caldina, Altezza, and Camry); **body panels, AWD driveline, and Super Strut parts are the hard, import-only part.**

## What Is the T200 Celica, and Why the GT-Four Matters

Toyota launched the sixth-generation Celica in **Japan in late 1993**, rolling out worldwide as a **1994** model. Visually it's unmistakable — soft, curvy bodywork and four round headlights that earned it a "bug-eye" nickname. It came as a **3-door liftback** (the common one), a **2-door coupe**, and a **convertible**.

But the reason enthusiasts still hunt the T200 is the **GT-Four (ST205)**, launched in Japan in **February 1994**. It's a homologation car: Toyota built it to go rallying, so it packs the **3S-GTE** turbocharged 2.0-liter and a proper **full-time AWD** system (viscous center diff, Torsen rear). This was Toyota Team Europe's WRC weapon — the ST205 era ended dramatically with a **1995 disqualification at the Rally Catalunya** over an illegal turbo restrictor, one of the sport's most infamous cheating scandals, which only adds to the car's notoriety today.

Everything else in the range orbits that halo. It's important to be honest about markets, though, because they diverged hard — and that matters for what you'll actually find as an import here.

## Engine & Spec Snapshot (by market)

| Trim / market | Engine | Output | Drive |
|---|---|---|---|
| ST (base) | 7A-FE 1.8L DOHC NA | ~105 hp | FWD |
| GT (US-market) | 5S-FE 2.2L DOHC NA | 135 hp / 145 lb-ft | FWD |
| SS-I/II/III (JDM) | 3S-GE 2.0L DOHC NA | ~180 PS (200 PS BEAMS, late '97) | FWD |
| GT-Four (ST205) | 3S-GTE 2.0L DOHC turbo | ~242 PS export / ~255 PS JDM | AWD |

A few honest caveats, because the internet muddles these:

- The **US never got the ST205 GT-Four** — America's turbo All-Trac ended with the previous ST185. So a US-spec T200 is a FWD 5S-FE GT at best; the turbo cars in PH are **JDM imports**.
- The base **7A-FE is more accurately ~105 hp**, not the 110 you'll sometimes see quoted. And keep the codes straight: **4A-FE is 1.6L, 7A-FE is 1.8L.**
- **US trim details** like standard dual front airbags, the "System 8" stereo, and EPA MPG figures are **US-market specifics** — don't assume a JDM import carries the same equipment; JDM content varied by trim.
- From **December 1997**, the JDM SS-II/SS-III and convertible switched to the **BEAMS 3S-GE (VVT-i)** — ~200 PS manual / 190 PS auto. This is the one **timing-belt-interference** engine in the range (see the FAQ), so it's worth identifying.

## Model Year Coverage

This guide covers the **T200 Celica, 1993/1994–1999** — the round-headlight generation. Within that run there was an **August 1995 facelift** (revised rear lamps and spoiler, new front bumper, JDM **SS-III** trim added), a **1996** export bumper change, and the **late-1997 BEAMS engine** swap for the top JDM NA trims. If a car has **angular, edgier styling and no turbo option**, you're looking at the **later T230 (1999–2006)** — a FWD-only, Yamaha-engined Celica (1ZZ/2ZZ) that's a different car and a different buying conversation. The quickest tell in a listing: **round headlights + a GT-Four/AWD badge = T200.**

## Common Upgrade Paths & Popular Kits

The T200's tuning story is really the **3S-GTE** story. Treat specific power figures as ballpark community results, not guarantees.

- **Bolt-ons first (GT-Four):** intake, a bigger front-mount intercooler, downpipe/exhaust, boost controller, and a proper ECU tune are the classic first steps — the 3S-GTE responds well and there's decades of MR2/Caldina knowledge to borrow from.
- **Deeper builds:** the platform's big advantage is **engine cross-pollination** — swapping in a later **Gen4 3S-GTE (from the Caldina)** or grafting on its parts is a well-trodden path, largely documented in the SW20 MR2 turbo community, whose 3S knowledge transfers directly. Big-turbo, standalone-ECU builds exist; the ceiling is high but so is the cost.
- **The Super Strut caveat (ST205):** many owners **delete the factory Super Strut front suspension in favor of conventional coilovers** — it's a widely endorsed reliability-and-cost move, not just a track hack (more on why below).
- **NA cars (SS / 5S-FE):** the 3S-GE responds to the usual header/intake/tune trio and handles beautifully on coilovers and an LSD, but it's not a forced-induction bargain the way the factory-turbo GT-Four is — if boost is the goal, start with a GT-Four.
- **PH reality:** FWD NA cars are the practical tuning base locally. A **full GT-Four AWD build is niche and expensive here** because the AWD driveline and Super Strut bits are import-only — budget for parts hunting, not just labor.

## Parts Availability & Sourcing in PH

Here's the honest split, because it's the difference between a fun project and a stalled one:

- **Engines and mechanical guts — obtainable.** The **3S family is shared widely** — 3S-GE and 3S-GTE with the **SW20 MR2**, the **Caldina** (Gen4 turbo), the **Altezza** (BEAMS), and the 5S-FE/3S-FE with **Camry/Carina** — so whole engines, internals, and service parts turn up through JDM importers and cross-compatible donors. Genuine catalog parts are still listed online (e.g. Amayama) split at the August 1995 change.
- **Body, AWD, and Super Strut — the hard part.** **Panels, the GT-Four AWD driveline, Super Strut components, and correct interior trim are scarce and largely import-only.** We could not confirm a reliable local (Banawe/Evangelista) supply specifically for Celica-unique parts — assume you'll import these and price accordingly.
- **Net:** keeping the engine alive is realistic; keeping a *rusty or crash-damaged* shell straight is where projects die. Buy the best body you can, because that's the part you can't easily replace here — the same lesson that makes a clean shell matter more than a clean engine on [the FWD AE92 Corolla](/blog/toyota-corolla-ae92-guide) too.

## Known Weak Points — Modified / Hard Use

Different risk profile from a stock daily. These are the recurring, corroborated themes:

- **Rust, first and always.** Floor pans (lift the carpet), strut towers, rockers, and quarter panels. On a humid, flood-exposed market this is the number-one killer — and structural rust is far more expensive to fix than any engine work.
- **GT-Four head gasket after overheating.** The 3S-GTE will let go at the head gasket if it's been cooked. The root cause is almost always **neglected cooling** — a tired radiator, old hoses, a lazy fan. Evidence of past overheating is a serious red flag on a turbo car.
- **Super Strut wear (ST205).** The clever factory front suspension is complex, wears out (often cited around 60,000–80,000 miles), and is expensive and hard to source — it shows up as knocking and odd tire wear. This is exactly why the conversion-to-standard-coilovers mod is so popular.
- **AWD driveline niggles.** Differential-mount failure (a clunk/play on the GT-Four) and an **expensive clutch** are the known AWD-specific costs. Budget for them.
- **Heat and age, not design.** High-rpm NA cars and boosted turbos both punish tired cooling systems; most "failures" on a 25-year-old car trace to deferred maintenance and history, not a fundamental flaw. Judge the individual car.

## Buying / Import Guide

Because it's an import-grade buy, due diligence is everything:

- **Hunt for rust before anything else.** Pull the carpets, check strut towers and the spare-tyre well, and be suspicious of fresh underseal hiding repairs. A rusty shell is a walk-away on a car whose panels you can't easily buy locally.
- **On a GT-Four, demand overheating/cooling and timing-belt history.** A cheap GT-Four with **no documented head-gasket and turbo service history** is a cheap GT-Four for a reason. Do a long (30-minute-plus) drive and watch the temperature under load.
- **Cold-start it** for knock and rattle, check oil for fuel smell or metal, and inspect the **Super Strut** for knocks and uneven tire wear.
- **Match the chassis number to the papers.** Verify the VIN/chassis stamping is intact and consistent, with no salvage/flood history hiding in a re-registered import.
- **Sort the import paperwork properly.** Documentation and legality for a used/gray import is a real hurdle in PH — we're not going to invent the exact process here; confirm the current requirements directly with the **LTO** (registration) and **Bureau of Customs** (import) before you hand over money, ideally with an importer who's done it before.

## Resale & Collector Trajectory — Set Expectations Honestly

The T200 splits sharply in two. The **ST205 GT-Four** is a genuine, appreciating collectible: it's rare (~2,500 built as a WRC homologation special), it carries real rally heritage, and clean, documented examples have crossed **$50,000** at international auction. That's a global trend, and it's well-documented.

The **FWD non-turbo T200s** (5S-FE, 3S-GE SS) are a different story — reliable, fun, but they appreciate minimally. They're weekend-car value, not investment-grade.

For the **Philippine market specifically, the value data is thin and anecdotal**, so we won't print a peso "market average" that a seller could quote back at you out of context. What's safe to say: a **verified, rust-free, documented GT-Four** is the one with a real appreciation case; everything else is worth what a clean, honest '90s coupe is worth. As always — buy the specific car in front of you, not the badge on the back of it.

## Verdict: The Last Celica That Rallied

The T200 earns its enthusiast status through one door — the **GT-Four** — and rewards anyone patient enough to find a straight, honest one. The engines are shareable and serviceable; the danger is the stuff you *can't* easily replace here: a rust-free shell, a healthy AWD driveline, and intact Super Strut (or a sensible coilover conversion). Get a clean example with real history and it's one of the most characterful, appreciating JDM turbo cars you can own in the Philippines. Chase a cheap, rusty, cooked one and it becomes the project that never ends.
