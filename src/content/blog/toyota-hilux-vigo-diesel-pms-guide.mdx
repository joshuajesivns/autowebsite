Not every Hilux on Philippine roads is a modern common-rail truck with a DPF. A huge share are the older **D-4D Vigo diesels** — the **2.5L 2KD-FTV** and **3.0L 1KD-FTV** engines that powered the Hilux from roughly **2005 to 2015** — and they're everywhere on the used market for good reason: matibay, murang parts, at kayang-kaya ng kahit anong talyer. But maintaining one is a *different animal* from the new Hilux. It's simpler in some ways (no DPF) and has one big cost the modern truck doesn't: a **timing belt**. Here's exactly what a classic Hilux diesel costs to keep healthy, what to watch on the KD engines, and what to check before you buy one. This builds on our [modern Hilux maintenance breakdown](/blog/toyota-hilux-pms-guide) and the [complete Philippine PMS and pricing guide](/blog/car-pms-philippines-guide).

## Key Takeaways

- **Timing belt, not chain.** The 2KD and 1KD D-4D use a **rubber timing belt** due for replacement around **150,000 km** — a ₱15,000–30,000+ job the modern chain-driven Hilux simply doesn't have. Budget for it.
- **No DPF.** These are pre-DPF engines, so no regeneration worries and **no low-ash DL-1 oil requirement** — conventional CF-grade diesel oil (e.g. 15W-40) is fine. Simpler and cheaper per service.
- **Cheaper routine PMS:** light PMS about **₱3,000–6,000**, heavy/major **₱8,000–15,000** — below the modern Hilux, mostly done at a talyer since these are out of warranty. *(Estimates, July 2026 — verify with your shop.)*
- **The big watch item is injectors.** Common-rail injector (and copper-seat) failure is the signature 1KD/2KD issue, especially on early units and dirty fuel — clean diesel and a healthy fuel filter are your best defense.
- **Two engines:** the economical **2.5L 2KD** (102–120 PS) and the stronger **3.0L 1KD** (163 PS). Both D-4D, both belt-driven, both DPF-free.
- **Still a smart used buy** — if the unit has records and a healthy top end. A well-kept Vigo is dependable; a neglected one gets expensive at the injectors and belt.

## Which Model Year Does This Guide Cover? (2005–2015, D-4D era)

This guide covers the **common-rail D-4D diesel Hilux** — principally the **seventh-generation Vigo (2005–2015)** with its **2.5L 2KD-FTV** and **3.0L 1KD-FTV** engines, plus the tail end of the sixth-generation "Tiger" (~2002–2004) that had already picked up the 2KD. These are the pre-DPF, belt-driven diesels that make up the bulk of the used Hilux market today.

Two things this guide deliberately does *not* cover in depth: the **all-new modern Hilux (2016 onward)**, whose GD-series diesels use a timing chain and a DPF — that's a different maintenance picture, handled in our [current Hilux service guide](/blog/toyota-hilux-pms-guide); and the **even older mechanical diesels** (the 2L/3L/5L indirect-injection engines and the electronically-controlled 1KZ-TE) found in earlier Tigers and before. Those are simpler still — no common-rail electronics at all — and deserve their own treatment rather than a few unverified lines here.

## Classic Hilux Maintenance Schedule (PMS Interval Chart)

The Vigo-era diesel still follows Toyota's familiar **every 5,000 km or 6 months** cadence — oil + filter and an inspection at each stop, with the bigger items landing at the 40,000 km and 80,000 km "heavy" services, and the timing belt as a separate milestone around 150,000 km:

| Odometer (km) | PMS type | Key items done |
|---|---|---|
| 5,000 | Light | Diesel engine oil + oil filter, general inspection |
| 10,000 | Light | Oil + filter, tire rotation, brake inspection |
| 20,000 | Light | Oil + filter, air-cleaner element check, fuel/water-separator drain |
| 30,000 | Light | Oil + filter, brake + suspension inspection |
| **40,000** | **Heavy / major** | Oil + filter, **fuel filter, brake fluid, coolant, air filter**, EGR + injector check, transmission + diff fluid check |
| 50,000–70,000 | Light | Oil + filter each 5k/6-mo stop, rotating inspections |
| **80,000** | **Heavy / major** | Everything at 40K **+ transmission fluid, transfer/diff oil (4x4)**, suspension + bushing inspection |
| **~150,000** | **Timing belt** | **Replace timing belt** (with tensioner/idler and water pump recommended) — separate from the regular PMS |

*Whichever comes first — km or months. No spark plugs (it's a diesel) and no DPF service — but do not skip the timing-belt milestone: on a belt engine, a snapped belt can mean serious engine damage. For dusty routes, hauling, or heavy traffic, treat the schedule as "severe" and never stretch the oil.*

## Light PMS vs. Heavy PMS on the Classic Hilux

Most visits are **light PMS** — diesel oil + filter and a general check, every 5,000 km. On these older common-rail engines, clean, on-time oil is the cheapest insurance you can buy: it protects the injectors, the VNT turbo, and the timing belt's tensioner.

The **heavy (major) PMS** at **40,000 km and 80,000 km** adds the fuel filter and water separator, brake fluid, coolant, air filter, and — importantly on the KD engines — an **EGR and injector check**, plus transmission and differential fluids (and transfer-case/diff oil on the 4x4). Then, standing apart from the regular schedule, is the **timing belt around 150,000 km** — do it with the tensioner, idler, and water pump while everything's open. Skipping the belt to save money is the one false economy that can turn into a rebuilt engine.

## Toyota Hilux D-4D: The Timing Belt Truth

This is the single most important thing that separates the classic Hilux from the new one. Both the **2.5L 2KD-FTV** and the **3.0L 1KD-FTV** use a **rubber timing belt**, not a chain. The generally cited replacement interval is **around 150,000 km** (some references note a belt-reminder light near 100,000 km — confirm what your specific unit calls for).

Why it matters so much: on a belt-driven diesel, a belt that snaps can let the valves and pistons collide — expensive engine damage, not just a tow. So on any used Vigo, **treat "when was the timing belt last done?" as a make-or-break question.** No record of a replacement on a 150,000 km+ truck? Assume it's overdue and get it done before you drive it hard. Have it replaced as a kit — belt, tensioner, idler pulley, and water pump together — since the labor to reach them is the bulk of the cost. This is precisely the scheduled expense the [modern chain-driven Hilux](/blog/toyota-hilux-pms-guide) was designed to eliminate.

## Known Issues on the Classic Hilux Diesel (2KD / 1KD)

These are genuinely reliable engines — many pass 200,000+ km — but they have a well-documented watch list, especially on the used market:

- **Injector failure — the signature issue.** The common-rail injectors (and the copper seat/washer beneath them) can fail, most commonly reported on **late 2004–2007 units** and on trucks fed low-quality diesel; failures have been seen from around 120,000 km. Symptoms include rough running, hard starting, and excess smoke. Clean fuel and an on-time fuel filter are the best prevention; injector service is the classic big repair on these.
- **EGR carbon buildup.** Worn injectors and lower-grade diesel accelerate carbon deposits in the intake, EGR valve, and combustion chamber — a common high-mileage complaint. Periodic EGR cleaning helps.
- **VNT turbo wear.** The variable-nozzle turbo can gum up or wear on high-mileage, neglected units — another reason clean oil and diesel matter.
- **Cracked pistons on Euro IV 1KD units (mostly export markets).** Some Euro IV 3.0L engines suffered cracked pistons between roughly 100,000–150,000 km (black smoke, knocking, high crankcase pressure, power loss); Toyota redesigned the pistons and oil jets in 2014. Philippine units of this era commonly ran to a lower emissions standard, so locally the **injector and EGR issues are the more frequently reported ones** — but it's worth knowing if you're looking at an imported unit.

None of these are reasons to avoid a Vigo diesel — they're reasons to buy one *with records* and to keep the fuel clean. A well-maintained example is one of the most dependable used trucks you can own.

## Casa vs. Talyer & Parts

Because these trucks are out of warranty, the practical choice is a **trusted, diesel-savvy talyer** over the casa — usually far cheaper for the same work, basta marunong sa common-rail D-4D at tama ang parts. The Hilux's biggest ownership advantage shows here: **parts are everywhere and cheap**, and virtually every mechanic in the country knows the KD engine. Between visits you can DIY the basics — oil-level checks, air and cabin filters, tire pressure — but leave injector work, timing-belt service, and EGR cleaning to someone who knows the D-4D. For a used unit, a one-time diagnostic at a diesel specialist before buying is money well spent.

## Verdict: A Smart Used Buy — If the Records Are There

The Vigo-era Hilux diesel earns its used-market popularity honestly: tough, cheap to feed and fix, and understood by every talyer in the country. Routine PMS is *cheaper* than the modern truck's — no DPF, no DL-1 oil — but you have to respect two things the new Hilux removed: the **timing belt around 150,000 km** and the **injector health** of an aging common-rail diesel. Used prices vary widely by year, engine, and condition, so shop on **service history and a healthy top end**, not just the odometer. Get those right and a classic Hilux will keep working long after trucks half its age have quit.
